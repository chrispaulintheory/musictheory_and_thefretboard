# Fretboard Biology — Pedagogical / Pacing Analysis

Notes on Joe Elliott's Fretboard Biology course (Levels 1–4, 10 units each), derived from the
unit LIST checklists (cumulative topic inventories) and Theory module PDFs. Purpose: mine pacing
ideas for Music Theory and The Fretboard Levels 2–3. Reference only — do not imitate his prose,
and do not let his voice leak into chapter drafting (see voice-profile.md).

## 1. Architecture: parallel strands, not a linear sequence

Every unit runs 5–7 **parallel strands simultaneously**: Theory, Fretboard Logic (his CAGED
"Octave Shape" system), Technique, Rhythm Notation, Rhythm Guitar, Improvisation, Practice.
A unit is a thin slice across all strands, not a chapter about one thing.

Per-unit dosage is strikingly small: **~1 new theory concept + 1–2 new fretboard patterns per
unit**. Everything else in the unit is consolidation of prior material. The strands advance
independently: theory can idle while fretboard logic advances, and vice versa.

Two recurring structures do heavy lifting:
- **"The List"** — every unit ends with a cumulative inventory of *everything taught so far*.
  It doubles as the source for the practice routine.
- **Practice module** — every unit closes with explicit practice-routine pedagogy: the routine
  as an ever-evolving document, alternating A/B routines on different days, regularity over
  binge practice, budgeting more time for new items, and criteria for *dropping* items
  (absorbed into newer skills).

## 2. The concept timeline (when he introduces what)

### Level 1 — notation, scales, keys (no chord theory at all)
| Unit | Theory | Fretboard |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Staff, clefs, musical alphabet; interval = distance (half/whole step only) | All 5 CAGED octave shapes, day one; notes on fretboard |
| 2 | Sharps/flats, enharmonics, naturally-occurring half steps, **major scale formula + major pentatonic** | Pattern III major pentatonic; chord tones of Pattern IV barre chord |
| 3 | **Natural minor + minor pentatonic** (derived from major by remove/add) | Pattern IV minor pentatonic |
| 4 | Scale building, sharp keys | Pattern I major pentatonic |
| 5 | Sharp key signatures | Pattern II minor pentatonic |
| 6 | Flat key signatures | Pattern III major scale; open voicings |
| 7 | All major key signatures | Pattern IV natural minor scale |
| 8 | **Parallel and relative keys** | Pattern I major scale |
| 9 | Minor key signatures (sharps) | Pattern II natural minor scale |
| 10 | All minor key signatures | Sus voicings |

Improvisation starts in **Unit 1** (solo-as-storytelling, "Five Elements of Contrast":
register, speed, complexity, density, volume) and key-center soloing over loops from Unit 2.

### Level 2 — intervals (rigorous), triads, harmonized scale, function
U1–2: interval quantity/quality, identification, construction (formal interval theory is
**delayed a full level** — until after a year of playing the scales those intervals live in).
U3: all four triad qualities at once (Maj/min/aug/dim, as a formula matrix) + sus4.
U4–5: triad analysis, chord symbols, construction. U6: **harmonized major scale with triads**.
U7: **chord families/function (tonic–subdominant–dominant, with "emotional effect")** +
harmonic analysis. U8: harmonized minor + minor-key analysis. U9: blues scale.
Fretboard: interval shapes drip-fed (2nds/3rds one string → adjacent strings → 4ths/5ths →
6ths → 7ths → per string-set); triad "Super Shapes"; arpeggios from U6.

### Level 3 — the same spiral again with 7th chords
U1: all four 7th qualities at once (ma7, 7, mi7, mi7♭5). U2: major scale harmonized in 7ths.
U3: chord families with 7ths. U4: progression-analysis reps. U5–6: harmonized minor in 7ths +
families. U7: analysis reps. U8–9: blues harmony (I7–IV7–V7 as its own system) + blues scale
vs. chord tones. Plus: "Money" modules (must-know licks), chord-tone soloing.

### Level 4 — modal interchange, inversions
U1–5: parallel keys → **modal interchange / borrowed chords** (triads, then 7ths) — note he
*re-teaches* parallel keys here even though they appeared in L1U8. U6–8: inversions, inverted
voicings, **slash chords**. U9–10: analysis with slash chords, consolidation.

## 3. Transferable pacing principles

1. **Hands before theory, by about a level.** Students play barre-chord chord tones in L1U2;
   the triad theory naming what their hands know arrives at L2U3. Theory lands on physical
   vocabulary that already exists, so it explains rather than introduces. (Our book does
   perception-before-symbol at the sentence level; FB does it at the *level* level.)
2. **Minor arrives almost immediately** (L1U3), taught as a cheap derivation from major
   (remove/add notes), because minor pentatonic is guitar-native. It then marinates for a
   whole level before minor *keys* are formalized (L1U8–10) and harmonized minor appears (L2U8).
3. **Function/families come one unit after harmonization, every time** (L2U6→U7 with triads,
   L3U2→U3 with 7ths). Harmonize the scale, then immediately teach tonic/subdominant/dominant
   roles and start analyzing real progressions.
4. **The harmonization spiral repeats verbatim at each chord level**: harmonize major → families
   → analyze reps → harmonize minor → families → analyze reps. Same lesson skeleton, new chord
   type. Predictability is the feature.
5. **Analysis practice gets its own units** (L3U4, L3U7 are almost pure progression-analysis
   reps). Applying the concept is scheduled as content, not left as an exercise afterthought.
6. **One-concept units + cumulative List.** New material per unit is tiny; the List makes the
   accumulation visible and feeds the practice routine.
7. **Just-in-time re-teaching.** Parallel keys taught in L1U8 are re-taught at L4U1 when modal
   interchange actually needs them. Early planting alone isn't trusted.
8. **Improvisation/application from day one**, on every unit's backing loop, with a framework
   (Five Elements of Contrast) that is *musical* rather than theoretical — so application never
   waits for theory to be "ready."
9. **Practice-routine pedagogy is taught explicitly and recurringly** — how to build, rotate,
   prune, and time-budget a routine; new items get more time; absorbed items get dropped.

## 4. Mapped to our book (Levels 2–3 recommendations)

- **Add a chord-function/families chapter immediately after "Harmonizing the C Major Scale"**
  in Level 2 (tonic/subdominant/dominant, emotional effect, primary/secondary chords as
  function). This is the clearest gap FB highlights: we harmonize the scale but don't yet say
  what the chords are *for*.
- **Follow every harmonization chapter with an analysis-reps chapter** ("here are 4 real
  progressions; find the key, label the numerals") — FB dedicates whole units to this and it's
  where the theory becomes usable.
- **Keep the L2/L3 spiral skeleton identical**: triads (harmonize major → function → analyze →
  minor) then 7ths in exactly the same order in L3. FB validates the book's existing spine and
  says: make the repetition of structure obvious, not disguised.
- **Consider a recurring "Musical Application" chapter cadence** (like the L1 Zombie chapter)
  every 4–6 chapters in L2, each over a real progression — FB's per-unit improv slot suggests
  application should recur, not appear once.
- **Add a cumulative "What you know so far" list** at part boundaries, doubling as the practice
  checklist feeding the BPM tracker.
- **Teach shapes slightly ahead of their theory** where possible (e.g., let readers play a
  triad voicing chapter before the full construction chapter names it) — FB's biggest
  structural trick.
- **Timing benchmarks**: sus chords bundled with triads (FB: L2U3; our L2 ToC already plans
  this — validated). Blues/borrowed-chord material deferred until after diatonic harmony is
  solid (FB waits until L3U8/L4 — validates deferring "chords from two keys"). Inversions: FB
  waits until L4 for inversion *voicings*/slash chords; our L2 does triad inversions earlier,
  which is defensible because our 3rds-first approach makes inversions cheap — but FB suggests
  slash-chord notation and inverted *voicings in progressions* can comfortably wait until L3.

## 5. What not to copy

- **Assertion-based theory.** FB defines and moves on (staff, clefs, key signatures as facts to
  memorize); almost nothing is derived or counted out. Our derivation-based approach is the
  differentiator — keep it.
- **The key-signature slog.** Seven of ten L1 theory units are scales/key signatures — thorough
  but grinding, and exactly the completionism our intro explicitly rejects.
- **Notation-first opening.** FB starts with the staff and both clefs before the student
  understands pitch. Our perception-first opening is stronger for the stated audience.
- **All-qualities-at-once matrices** (4 triad types in one unit, 4 seventh-chord types in one
  unit). It works for FB only because the hands are a year ahead; with our tighter
  reading-order coupling, one-quality-at-a-time derivation carries less load.

## 6. Decision (2026-07-06): scales in Level 2

Context: Level 1 deliberately stays on the E-A-D string set, so scales beyond the
single-string 221-2221 presentation were out of scope. Level 2 opens up all six strings.
Plan for where scales enter, anchored to the current `reorder_level2.py` ORDER (23 chapters):

1. **Ch 11.5 — "The Fretboard: The C Major Scale in Open Position"** (insert between
   "Memorizing the G and B Strings" and "The 5th, 4th, and 3rd Shapes on the Treble Strings").
   - It cashes in the G/B/e note memorization immediately (FB principle: every knowledge
     investment pays off within a unit).
   - Derive, don't assert: *fold* the L1 single-string scale across the strings; the fold
     behaves differently at the G→B corner, reinforcing the tuning anomaly taught in chs 10–12.
   - Callback: the L1 standard-notation bonus chapters already taught these exact notes
     (including the one-octave C scale).
   - Use true open position (open strings), presented E-to-G rainbow ("every scale note in
     open position") before extracting the C-to-C octave. Natural fretboard-generator figure.
   - Later chapters (Triad Arpeggio, harmonized 3rds on treble strings, improvisation)
     silently depend on scale-across-strings ability; nothing currently provides it.
2. **Adjacent to ch 17 (The Triad Arpeggio) — the movable major-scale pattern around the
   E-shape barre chord.** Framing: the arpeggio shows the chord tones inside the shape; the
   scale shows the rest of the palette around the same shape; slide the barre and the scale
   slides with it. **Two patterns total in L2** (open + one movable). Do NOT teach the full
   five-pattern/CAGED scale system in L2 — FB drips one pattern per unit and needs three
   levels to finish the system; the full system waits for Level 3 (7th-chord arpeggios will
   need it).
3. **After ch 23 ("The Rest of the Chords in the Key") — the minor scale, via the PARALLEL
   comparison on D** (decision 2026-07-06; full chapter plan in repo-root `minor.md`).
   - The reader now owns minor *chords* (Am, Dm, Em) but no minor *scale* — the gap is felt.
   - Parallel D major → D minor isolates the quality difference for the ear, and lowering 6̂
     (B → B♭) forces the book's **first flat** as the climax of the same constraint
     derivation that birthed F♯ in L1. Relative keys (D minor ↔ F major; C ↔ Am as the
     confirming example; the La doorway) move to the F major chapter that follows.
   - Do not push minor past Level 2 (FB has it in L1U3; guitarists crave minor pentatonic).
4. **Then a "Musical Application" chapter**: improvise a melody over a I–vi–IV–V loop in C
   using the open-position scale — L2's counterpart to L1's Zombie chapter.
5. **Practice scaffolding**: give both scale patterns BPM goals in the progress tracker
   (mirroring L1's "all 7 naturals as quarter notes at 80 BPM" criterion).
6. **Guardrail**: no scale pattern before the G/B memorization chapter — introduced earlier,
   it degrades into a rote finger shape (the "packaging without the product" failure mode the
   book's intro criticizes).

Net insertion map: ch 11.5 open-position C major → ch 17-adjacent movable pattern (E-shape) →
post-ch 23 minor scale/relative keys + open-position minor pentatonic → application chapter.
