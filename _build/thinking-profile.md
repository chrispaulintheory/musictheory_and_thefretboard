# Thinking Profile — Music Theory and The Fretboard (Level 1)

A reverse-engineering of the author's *cognitive* style — reasoning patterns, explanatory
framework, assumptions about learning, argument construction, decision-making — as evidenced by
Level 1. Companion to `voice-profile.md` (which covers the prose surface).

**Core thesis:** The author thinks like a systems engineer with an empiricist epistemology.
A fact isn't believed until it can be recomputed from a smaller set of primitives, and it isn't
considered *taught* until the learner can recompute it too. Teaching, in this mind, is not
transferring outputs — it is transferring the generator.

---

## 1. How you think (reasoning patterns)

### 1.1 Verification over authority
Nothing in the book rests on "because that's the rule." Every claim is grounded in one of three
courts of appeal, in descending order of preference:
1. **Nature/physics** — frequencies, doubling, the harmonic series, Pythagoras' monochord.
2. **Arithmetic** — counted out in full, every time ("B (0), C (1)… that's only 6 half steps").
3. **Direct perception** — "you can hear it," "feel it in your throat," "watch your foot."

Authorities (Copland, Elson's dictionary, Open Music Theory, Hutchinson) are cited as
*calibration*, never as proof: "Going Deeper" says "here is the rigorous version I simplified
from," not "believe me because they said so."

### 1.2 First-principles reduction
You habitually dig beneath the received framing to the generative rule:
- "Strictly speaking, a chord is built from intervals, not from a scale."
- The tuning's G–B anomaly is *derived* from interval logic, not memorized.
- Note positions come from pitch arithmetic, never from fixed shapes.
- The product/packaging distinction: theory is the invariant; CAGED and shapes are representations.

The recurring mental question is: *what is the smallest set of primitives from which everything
else follows?* (Level 1's answer: pitch → half step → interval → everything.)

### 1.3 Invariants and transformations
The mind gravitates to what stays the same under change: the 221-2221 formula invariant under
transposition; movable shapes; octave equivalence; inversion pairs summing to 9 (names) and
12 (half steps). Concepts are considered understood when their behavior under transformation
is known.

### 1.4 The exception as the most informative data point
B–F (the diminished 5th) gets two full chapters. E♯-not-F gets a "watch out." The anomaly is
never an annoyance to be waved off — it's the test case that proves you understand the rule.
Boundary conditions are actively sought: fret 0, the 12th fret, the 7th degree.

### 1.5 Pattern → conjecture → verify
The signature reasoning loop, performed openly: "C has 0 sharps. G has 1. Therefore, D must have
2 sharps? Let's check… yes, the pattern fits!" Inductive leap, then deductive verification —
and the verification is never skipped.

### 1.6 Constraint-satisfaction arguments
F♯ is not asserted; it is *forced*: (a) all 7 letter names must appear exactly once, (b) the
221-2221 pattern must hold — therefore the note must be F♯ and nothing else. This "show the
solution is the unique one satisfying the constraints" move is a favorite argument form.

### 1.7 Quantification instinct, with fencepost vigilance
Qualitative phenomena are converted to countable quantities before being reasoned about
(intervals become two numbers: quantity and quality). Off-by-one hazards are treated as a
first-class danger — count-from-0 vs. count-from-1 is explicitly modeled (board-game analogy;
"13 notes but only 12 names"). This is a programmer's scar tissue.

### 1.8 Disambiguation as a core drive
Wherever one word has two referents, or two things share a name, enormous care is spent:
up/down the fretboard (pitch vs. physical orientation), scale degrees vs. intervals, string
numbers running opposite to pitch order, half rest vs. whole rest. The implicit theory:
**most confusion in the world is ambiguity, not difficulty.**

### 1.9 Taxonomy and naming
Concepts are sorted into families with explicit membership lists (Perfect Interval Family;
Imperfect Family) and attributes (quantity/quality). When standard vocabulary has a gap, you
coin a term and flag the coinage ("A 'harmonic zone' is a term I use"). Naming is treated as a
tool of thought, not decoration.

### 1.10 Both-and integration
Debates are resolved by finding the frame in which both camps are right, then layering:
shortcuts AND fundamentals; theory AND shapes; "the product" AND "the packaging." You rarely
pick a side; you build a hierarchy that contains both sides.

## 2. How you teach (pedagogical framework)

### 2.1 Cognitive load is the enemy
The central design constraint, stated outright: "maximize efficiency and minimize overwhelm."
It drives every scope decision: modes excluded on purpose; only the bass strings ("it's only
half of the fretboard and therefore is not as overwhelming"); only 12 frets; one note at a time;
power chords before triads because "there are only two notes to worry about."

### 2.2 Sequencing is the primary lever
"There's no new information in this book that you can't find elsewhere. However, I've carefully
put together the information in a specific order." The self-image is architect/curator, not
inventor: value lives in *arrangement*, and the ordering is load-bearing (you can't understand
chapter N's explanation without chapter N−2's installed metaphor).

### 2.3 Perception before symbol
"You don't have to know the names of the notes just yet to understand this concept because you
can hear it." Sound and body first (larynx, foot-tap, hearing octaves "just fit"), names and
notation second. Symbols are treated as labels for experiences the learner already had.

### 2.4 Dual (or triple) coding
Every concept is deliberately encoded in multiple representations: abstract + fretboard (the
book's chapter-pair skeleton); numeric (221-2221) + mnemonic (phone number) + symbolic (WWH) +
visual (rainbow); auditory + kinesthetic. The working definition of understanding is
**the ability to translate between representations.**

### 2.5 Misconceptions are predictable, so pre-empt them
You model the learner's mind continuously and maintain a catalog of known failure modes
(harvested from Reddit, YouTube, and the classroom): degrees-vs-intervals conflation, the
counting-from-1 trap, verbal count-off faster than playing tempo. Guards are installed *before*
the error occurs ("Here's the part that trips people up").

### 2.6 Motivation is engineered, not assumed
Every effortful task gets an explicit payoff ("memorize the bass strings → you'll instantly know
the name of every chord"), and boring drills get a fun application bolted on (the Zombie
power-chord exercise exists because "the memorization exercise can be a bit boring").
The theory of change: "confidence... comes from a solid foundation of competence" — confidence
is an *output* of mastery, never a pep-talk input.

### 2.7 Partial understanding is legitimate scaffolding
Forward promises ("I'll explain why later," "you don't need to read this notation yet") show
comfort with provisional knowledge. Definitions are allowed to be temporarily incomplete as long
as the debt is tracked and repaid — a spiral curriculum: introduce lightly, return with rigor.

### 2.8 Mastery must be measurable
Fuzzy goals are operationalized: the BPM progress tracker; "all 7 natural notes as quarter notes
at 80 BPM without any errors"; "a specific strategy that has a 100% success rate." Practice is
specified like a protocol (which finger, which fret, what to say aloud).

### 2.9 Selective omission as a teaching act
"I purposefully exclude some concepts" — leaving things out is treated as a positive pedagogical
decision requiring courage ("it seems some authors are afraid of being criticized for leaving
some stuff out"), not a gap.

## 3. How you write (surface expression — see voice-profile.md)

The prose habits are downstream consequences of the layers above:

| Writing habit | Teaching decision it serves | Thinking pattern beneath it |
|---|---|---|
| Counting performed in-line: "C (1), D (2)…" | learner must be able to verify | verification over authority |
| "Lighter shade" reused for a dozen chapters | one metaphor, amortized load | invariants; dual coding |
| "Here's the part that trips people up" | pre-empt the known misconception | learner-mind modeling |
| Equation headings ("quarter note = one beat") | definition before discussion | taxonomy/naming as thought |
| "Strictly speaking… but the shortcut is useful" | keep trust while correcting | both-and integration |
| "Let's check… yes, the pattern fits!" | model how to learn, not just what | conjecture → verify loop |
| "Going Deeper" citation blocks | honesty about simplification | authorities as calibration |
| Short-sentence reveals ("They do not.") | mark the load-bearing fact | knowing which fact is generative |

## 4. Preferred analogies (and what they reveal)

Analogies are chosen for **structural isomorphism**, not vibe — they are functional models the
learner can compute with: color/shade preserves the actual structure of octave equivalence
(same hue, different lightness = same pitch class, different octave); the board game encodes the
off-by-one rule; the fraction reading *derives* the time signature's meaning; the phone number is
a pure mnemonic and is used only as one. Source domains are exclusively perception (color,
sound), the body (nose/toes, foot, heartbeat, larynx), everyday numeracy (fractions, phone
numbers, board games), and nature (constellations, octopus) — never machinery, sports, war, or
business. The world model is sensory and domestic, and the reader is assumed to share it.

## 5. Decision-making process (visible in the intro and design choices)

1. **Empirical/pragmatic**: five years of classroom iteration; kept what demonstrably worked
   ("we were strumming along to Hotel California… it was a very popular class because we had fun!").
2. **Explicit tradeoff articulation**: the NAfME classical path was considered and rejected with
   stated reasons (time, prerequisites, class goals) — options are weighed on record, not silently.
3. **Ruthless prioritization / 80-20 instinct**: "focus on the most relevant information that
   will give you the most results possible."
4. **Design by constraint**: decisions justified by what they make impossible (excluding modes
   prevents overwhelm) as much as by what they enable.
5. **Reputation risk accepted for scope discipline**: willing to be criticized for omission.

## 6. Blind spots and tensions

1. **You universalize your own cognitive style.** The book assumes every learner is bothered by
   unexplained rules and needs the mechanism (order of sharps is *derived* when it could be
   memorized in 10 seconds). The intro even selects for such readers ("you rely on shapes…
   but you want a better understanding"). Learners who are happy with rote get no path.
2. **The disambiguation drive sometimes serves the author's need for closure**, not the learner:
   the up/down-the-fretboard digression resolves an ambiguity many beginners never noticed,
   at real length.
3. **Completionist vs. minimalist tension**: the stated plan is "theory chapters light on words,"
   but anomalies can't be left unexplained (B–F gets two chapters). The exception-hunting
   instinct fights the load-management instinct, and usually wins.
4. **Redundancy as insurance**: "In other words" restatements suggest low trust that a single
   explanation landed — reasonable classroom instinct, but on the page it occasionally buries
   the point it is protecting.

## 7. Summary: think / teach / write in one line each

- **Think:** reduce to primitives, quantify, hunt the invariant and the exception, conjecture
  then verify, never accept a rule you can't re-derive.
- **Teach:** transfer the generator, not the outputs — sequence ruthlessly, ground perception
  before symbols, encode everything twice, pre-empt the predictable error, and make mastery
  measurable.
- **Write:** perform the reasoning on the page in short, second-person sentences, pointing at
  the shared diagram, with a small set of installed metaphors doing the heavy lifting.
