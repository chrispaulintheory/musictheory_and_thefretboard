Music Theory: The Minor Scale

You have played minor chords for a while now. You know Dm, Em, and Am as chords inside the key of C. You also know that one lowered 3rd can turn a Major chord dark.

Now we are going to do the same thing to a whole scale.

A minor scale is a seven-note palette with a darker pattern than the Major scale. We will build it from D because you already know D Major from Level 1, and because the comparison is clean: same home note, different color.

Start with D Major

Here is the D Major scale you built in Level 1:

D, E, F♯, G, A, B, C♯, D

Its scale degrees are:

Degree12345671
D MajorDEF♯GABC♯D

Major uses the 221-2221 pattern. Count the half steps and it checks out: D to E is 2, E to F♯ is 2, F♯ to G is 1, and so on.

Lower 3, 6, and 7

To turn D Major into D minor, keep the same home note and lower three degrees by one half step: 3, 6, and 7.

First, lower 3. F♯ drops one half step to F. The sharp comes off.

Next, lower 7. C♯ drops one half step to C. Again, the sharp comes off.

Those two are easy because D Major already had sharps to remove. But 6 is different. The 6th degree in D Major is B. Lower B by one half step and you cannot call it A♯, because the scale already has an A. A seven-note scale must use each letter name once: D, E, F, G, A, B, C.

So a new symbol is forced.

A flat lowers a note by one half step. B lowered by one half step is B♭. The flat is the mirror image of the sharp: a sharp raises, a flat lowers.

Degree12345671
D MajorDEF♯GABC♯D
D minorDEFGAB♭CD

There is the D minor scale: D, E, F, G, A, B♭, C, D.

The minor pattern

Now count the half steps in D minor.

  • D to E = 2
  • E to F = 1
  • F to G = 2
  • G to A = 2
  • A to B♭ = 1
  • B♭ to C = 2
  • C to D = 2

So the minor scale pattern is 212-2122. Its half steps live between 2-3 and 5-6, instead of Major's 3-4 and 7-1.

That is why it sounds different. Same tonic, different half-step locations.

Parallel scales

Two scales are parallel when they share the same tonic. D Major and D minor are parallel because both use D as home.

This is the cleanest way to hear the Major/minor difference. Play D Major, then D minor. Do not change home base. Only change the palette around that home base.

The lowered 3rd does the most obvious darkening, because it changes the chord built on home from D Major to D minor. The lowered 6th and 7th finish the color of the whole scale.

Hear it

Sing or play just the first five notes of each scale:

  • D Major: D, E, F♯, G, A
  • D minor: D, E, F, G, A

Only one note changed, but the mood changes immediately. That is the 3rd doing its old job, now at scale size.

Try it yourself

Answer these without looking back.

  1. Which three degrees are lowered to turn a Major scale into a minor scale?
  2. Why is the lowered 6th degree of D called B♭ instead of A♯?
  3. What is the minor scale half-step pattern?
Answer key
  • Lower 3, 6, and 7.
  • Because the scale must use each letter name once. A is already degree 5, so the lowered B must still be some kind of B: B♭.
  • 212-2122.

You now have the minor scale generator: same home note, lower 3, 6, and 7, and count 212-2122 to verify it.
