Level 3 · Chapter 3

Melodic Shape = The Flow of Energy

So far, we have learned how phrasing shapes a melody through entrances, endings, duration, and silence.

Now we turn to the second core component: melodic shape.

Melodic shape is the path a listener hears as a line rises, falls, or holds its ground.

That path contributes to the melody’s sense of movement and energy.

What Is Contour?

“Contour” is the path a melody takes as it moves through pitch.

A line can ascend, descend, or move horizontally.

Horizontal movement occurs when a pitch is repeated or sustained, creating a level section in the contour.

These three movements help energy build, hold, and release.

Three Basic Movements

Listen for these three movements whenever you study a melody.

Ascending = Building Energy

A rising melody often creates increasing energy and anticipation.

It can feel as though the line is moving toward an important moment.

Rhythm, dynamics, harmony, and tempo also contribute to the result, so the effect will vary with the musical context.

Horizontal = Holding Energy

A melody moves horizontally when it repeats a pitch or sustains one across time.

The contour holds its level while rhythm, harmony, tone, or dynamics continue moving around it.

A horizontal section gives the ear a stable point to follow and creates contrast with rising and falling shapes.

Descending = Releasing Energy

A descending line often releases energy and can feel like landing or arriving home.

Rhythm, harmony, dynamics, and phrase placement determine the strength of that effect.

Question and Answer Can Be Contour

One of the easiest ways to hear contour is to compare two phrases with the same rhythm.

The first phrase might rise and feel open, unfinished, or expectant.

The second phrase might fall and feel settled, closed, or complete.

The shared timing makes the effect of contour easier to hear.

Range Changes the Shape

Range is the distance between the lowest and highest notes in a melody.

Contour describes the path of the line. Range describes the vertical space that path occupies. Together, they give us a fuller picture of melodic shape.

When every phrase remains inside the same one-octave range, the melody can begin to feel boxed in, or “stuffy.” The listener becomes familiar with its ceiling and floor, and later phrases may seem to circle the same space.

Letting a later line continue beyond the octave can create lift, expansion, or arrival.

The effect comes from contrast. A melody establishes a familiar range, moves beyond it when the music needs more energy, and can return when that energy settles.

Establish the rangeExtend beyond the octaveReturn or continue

Listen for the Change in Range

To isolate the effect of range, compare two versions with the same harmony, tempo, tone, phrase boundaries, and basic musical idea.

  • Version A: The melody remains compressed inside one octave.
  • Version B: The line continues beyond the octave and establishes a new high point.

Listen for the moment the second version crosses the previous ceiling. Does it feel more open? Does the new range create lift, expansion, or a stronger arrival?

Common Melodic Shapes

Most melodies are built from just a handful of basic shapes.

Here are some of the most common.

Ascending

The melody gradually climbs.

This builds energy.

Descending

The melody gradually falls.

This releases energy.

Arch

The melody climbs...

reaches a high point...

then comes back down.

Many memorable melodies use this shape because it naturally creates tension and release.

Wave

The melody rises and falls several times.

Like gentle waves in the ocean.

The energy keeps moving without becoming predictable.

Horizontal

The melody repeats or sustains a pitch, creating a level section in the contour.

This can create focus, stability, and contrast with the shapes around it.

Repetition of Shape

A melody can repeat a contour without repeating the exact notes.

A sequence develops an idea by repeating its shape from a new starting pitch. The repeated idea may itself ascend, descend, form an arch, or follow another contour.

For example, a melody might climb through three notes and then repeat that rising shape from a new starting pitch.

The pitches change while the familiar contour creates unity.

Exact repetition establishes a strong pattern. Small variations keep that pattern developing.

From Monotony to Variation

When studying a professional melody, begin with the assumption that the line already makes sense within its genre.

Listen for how the melody varies an established shape.

A repeated contour can establish the melody’s identity.

Variation can then change the direction, move the high point, add a horizontal section, or expand the range.

Listen for the moment each change appears and the effect it creates.

Listen for Melodic Shape

Choose a short passage from a professionally recorded melody or solo and listen to it several times.

Listening Assignment

  1. Trace the contour with your hand as the melody rises and falls.
  2. Notice every repeated or sustained pitch that creates horizontal movement.
  3. Estimate the distance between the lowest and highest notes.
  4. Listen for any moment when the line extends beyond its established range.
  5. Identify one contour that returns and describe how a later version varies it.

Use these questions during the final listen:

  • Where does the melody rise, fall, or move horizontally?
  • Where does a repeated or sustained pitch create contrast?
  • Does the melody remain within one octave?
  • Where does a wider range create lift, expansion, or arrival?
  • Which shape becomes familiar, and how is it varied later?

Chapter Summary

  • Melodic shape is the path a listener hears.
  • Ascending shapes often build energy.
  • Repeated or sustained pitches create horizontal movement.
  • Descending shapes often release energy.
  • Range describes the distance between a melody’s lowest and highest notes.
  • Moving beyond an established octave can create lift and expansion.
  • Professional melodies often establish a recognizable shape and introduce variation over time.
