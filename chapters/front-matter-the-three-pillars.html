The Three Pillars: How Music (and This Book) Fits Together

Before we start, here is the whole map on one page. Aaron Copland (whom I mentioned a moment ago) named four elements of music: rhythm, melody, harmony, and tone color (timbre). Tone color—why a guitar and a trumpet sound different on the same note—is wonderful, but it isn’t something you build a song out of. So this book focuses on the three elements you actually compose with:

Harmony. Melody. Rhythm.

Everything in this book serves one of those three. And each pillar breaks down into three simple parts:

Harmony — notes played together (the chords under a song)

  • Chord construction — what notes make a chord. We start with the simplest one possible: the power chord (just a root and a 5th).
  • Chords in a key — which chords belong together, so they sound good in the same song.
  • Progressions & voicings — putting chords in an order that moves, and choosing where on the neck to play them.

Melody — notes played in sequence (the part you sing)

  • Phrasing — where a musical line breathes; where it begins and ends.
  • Melodic shape — the contour of a line, its rise and fall.
  • Note choice — which notes you pick against the underlying harmony. This is where scales and intervals pay off.

Rhythm — where every note sits in time (the pillar the other two stand on)

  • Beat & tempo — the pulse, and how fast it goes.
  • Note duration — how long each note is held; long notes versus short ones.
  • Meter — how beats group together (time signatures, and counting along).

Where the “theory” fits

You may be wondering where things like note names, the fretboard, intervals, and reading notation live in this picture. They aren’t a fourth pillar—they are the shared toolkit that all three pillars draw on. Knowing the notes and the fretboard is what lets you build a chord (harmony), choose a note (melody), and place it in time (rhythm). That is why we cover them first: they are the tools, not the goal.

Why we start with power chords

Most theory books begin harmony with the triad (a three-note chord). We start one rung lower, with the power chord—two notes, one shape, and no “major or minor” quality to reason about yet. It is the simplest possible chord, and you can play real songs with it almost immediately. Once power chords are second nature, a triad is just a power chord with one note added. We climb the ladder one rung at a time, on purpose, so nothing ever feels like a leap.

Keep these three pillars in the back of your mind as you read. Every chapter is a tool for, or a piece of, Harmony, Melody, or Rhythm.
