Music Theory: Inverting the Imperfect Intervals

Chapter objective. Invert a Major or minor 3rd into the correct 6th and explain both inversion rules: numbers total 9, and Major/minor trade places.

Retrieve first. Write a Major 3rd and a minor 3rd above C. Without looking, predict the number and quality of each inversion.

Review point. The prediction becomes a fretboard shape in Chapter 21 and returns in the Unit 5 checkpoint in Chapter 25.

Back in Level 1 you learned how to invert an interval: take the lower note and flip it up top. The Perfect 5th G–D became the Perfect 4th D–G, and two rules fell out of the flip. The interval numbers always add up to 9, and a Perfect interval stays Perfect. Now you own a second family, the imperfect intervals, and it is time to flip those too.

Invert the Major 3rd

Start with the Major 3rd you know best: G up to B, 4 half steps. Flip it. The G jumps up an octave, and the interval is now B up to G.

Measure it the way you measure every interval: quantity first, then quality. B up to G spans 6 note names: B (1), C (2), D (3), E (4), F (5), G (6). So it is some kind of 6th, and the first rule checks out: a 3rd inverted into a 6th, and 3 + 6 = 9. Now count the half steps from B up to G:

8 half steps. Like every imperfect interval, the 6th comes in two everyday sizes: the Major 6th traverses 9 half steps, and the minor 6th traverses 8. So B–G is a minor 6th. Look closely at what just happened: the Major 3rd flipped into a minor 6th.

Invert the minor 3rd

Now the other one: E up to G, 3 half steps, the minor 3rd. Flip it and you get G up to E.

G up to E spans 6 note names, G (1), A (2), B (3), C (4), D (5), E (6), and traverses 9 half steps. A Major 6th. The minor 3rd flipped into a Major 6th.

The third rule: Major and minor trade places

That is not a coincidence. An interval and its inversion always add up to exactly one octave, 12 half steps. The Major 3rd (4) plus the minor 6th (8) is 12. The minor 3rd (3) plus the Major 6th (9) is 12. The two pieces have to share those 12 half steps between them, so the bigger the piece you start with, the smaller the piece left over. Major is the bigger size of any imperfect interval, so a Major interval always leaves behind the smaller, minor-sized remainder, and a minor interval leaves the bigger, Major-sized one.

Here is the complete set of inversion rules, old and new:

  • The interval numbers add up to 9: a 5th flips to a 4th, a 3rd flips to a 6th, a Unison flips to an Octave.
  • A Perfect interval stays Perfect.
  • Major and minor trade places.
  • Diminished and Augmented trade places (the Diminished 5th and Augmented 4th, from Level 1).

Hear it

  • The Major 6th: play the open G string, then the 5th fret of the B string (E). G up to E, wide and warm.
  • The minor 6th: play the open B string, then the 3rd fret of the high e string (G). B up to G, one half step narrower, darker and sweeter.
  • An interval and its flip: play E up to G on the low E string (open, then the 3rd fret): the minor 3rd, small and moody. Then play G up to E from the Major 6th example above. The same two letters, turned over, and the color turns over with them.

Try it yourself

Invert each 3rd below. Name the notes of the new interval, count its half steps (the ruler can help), and call it a Major 6th or a minor 6th. The first one starts like this: C–E inverts to E–C.

  1. C–E (a Major 3rd)
  2. A–C (a minor 3rd)
  3. F–A (a Major 3rd)
  4. D–F (a minor 3rd)
Answer key
  1. E–C: 8 half steps, a minor 6th
  2. C–A: 9 half steps, a Major 6th
  3. A–F: 8 half steps, a minor 6th
  4. F–D: 9 half steps, a Major 6th

Notice that you never really had to count. Major flips to minor and minor flips to Major, every single time.

Every 3rd you own is a 6th seen from the other side. Flip the notes and the quantity flips around 9, the quality trades Major for minor, and the two pieces always close up a perfect octave. You measured two new intervals today without meeting a single new note.
