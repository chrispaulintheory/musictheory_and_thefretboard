Chapter 46

Major, Minor, and Diminished Triads

In chapter 1, we discussed the Major and Minor 3rd interval. Understanding these two intervals will be the foundation for having a solid understanding of the triad. To review—

Major and Minor Triads

The Major triad is a Major 3rd + a Minor 3rd. The Minor triad is reversed—a Minor 3rd + a Major 3rd. Take a look at these two examples.

The first Major triad built is called “G Major” and its chord symbol is just “G”. The next triad is a Minor triad and it is called “E Minor”. Its chord symbol is “Em”.

I’m using these two examples because the G Major triad is found in all three keys that we have studied so far. It is the V chord in the key of C, the I chord in the key of G, and the IV chord in the key of D. Therefore we know that a Major triad is not defined by the Major scale but rather the intervals that make up the chord.

The same goes for the E Minor triad. It is the iii chord in the key of C, the vi chord in the key of G, and the ii chord in the key of D.

When online guitar teachers recommend their viewers to think of the G Major scale to create the G Major triad, they are giving you a shortcut. A shortcut is indeed useful! But now you have a much more solid understanding of triads and how they fit within a key.
