Level 2 · Chapter 35

The Fretboard: The F Major Scale

Chapter objective. Play and spell F Major in open position, use B♭ accurately, and connect F Major's chord map to the same palette heard as D natural minor.

Retrieve first. Write F Major and D natural minor without looking. Underline the shared notes, then circle the different tonic in each line.

Assessment point. Complete the Unit 7 checkpoint at the end of this chapter before starting the two closing theory chapters and final capstone.

F Major is the first Major scale in this book with a flat. Its notes are F, G, A, B♭, C, D, E, F.

The good news is that the flat is not mysterious on the guitar. It is just one fret lower than B.

So let's find it.

F Major in open position

Here is a one-octave F Major scale starting on the low E string:

Degree12345671
NoteFGAB♭CDEF
String/fretE 1E 3A 0A 1A 3D 0D 2D 3

Play it as frets 1, 3 on the low E; 0, 1, 3 on the A; 0, 2, 3 on the D.

Notice the B♭. It sits on the A string, 1st fret, right between open A and the 2nd-fret B you memorized back in Level 1. The flat did exactly what the symbol promised: it lowered B by one half step.

The same palette as D minor

Now compare F Major to the D minor scale from the last fretboard chapter:

F MajorFGAB♭CDEF
D minorDEFGAB♭CD

Same notes, different starting point. If you practice both in open position, you are really learning one shared neighborhood with two homes.

The main chords in F Major

F Major's primary triads are I, IV, and V. Count the scale degrees: F (1), G (2), A (3), B♭ (4), C (5). So the primary chords are:

  • I = F
  • IV = B♭
  • V = C

On the fretboard, that means your familiar I–IV–V shape pattern can start at F, the 1st fret of the low E string. I is F as an E-shape barre. IV is B♭ as an A-shape barre at the 1st fret. V is C as an A-shape barre at the 3rd fret.

Again, the B♭ chord at the 1st fret is physically demanding. Use the 6th-fret E-shape B♭ when you need an easier grip. Same chord, different home on the neck.

A four-chord loop in F

The I–V–vi–IV loop works in F too. First find vi. Count to the 6th degree: F (1), G (2), A (3), B♭ (4), C (5), D (6). The vi chord is Dm.

NumeralsIVviIV
Key of FFCDmB♭

Play it slowly: F → C → Dm → B♭. You have heard that shape of motion before. The new key simply spells it with one flat.

Hear it

Play the F Major scale up and down. Then play D minor up and down. Do not change the note collection. Change the note that feels like home.

That is the fretboard version of relative keys: one shared set of notes under your fingers, two centers of gravity for your ear.

Track your progress

Add F Major in open position to the Progress Tracker: ascending and descending, quarter notes at 80 BPM without errors. Say the flat out loud every time: B♭, not B.

Try it yourself

Cover the table and answer these from the fretboard.

  1. Where is B♭ in the open-position F Major scale?
  2. What are I, IV, and V in F Major?
  3. What is the vi chord in F Major?
Answer key
  • A string, 1st fret.
  • I = F, IV = B♭, V = C.
  • vi = Dm.

One flat is now part of your fretboard map. F Major and D minor are no longer separate facts; they are the same palette, heard from two homes.

Unit 7 checkpoint: one palette, two homes

Do this before moving into the closing theory review. It is deliberately a transfer task, not another read-the-diagram exercise.

  1. Write F Major and D natural minor from memory. Mark B♭ in both, then circle F in one scale and D in the other as the different tonics.
  2. Play F → C → Dm → B♭ at a steady four beats per chord. State the Roman numerals in F Major as you change.
  3. Play Dm → B♭ → C → Dm. State the Roman numerals in D natural minor and explain why the same C chord has a different role from the C chord in the F-Major loop.
  4. Over either loop, make a two-measure melody using the shared note collection. Land on a chord tone on the first beat of each chord.
Pass rule and closing-sequence preparation

Pass when the spelling is correct, B♭ is played rather than B, the two tonal centers are named accurately, and the melody begins each change on a current chord tone. If not, repair the failed task at the next practice session. After three more sessions, repeat it without preparation; that is the readiness check for the capstone.
