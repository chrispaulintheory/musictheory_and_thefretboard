Chapter 44

Triads from the 30,000-foot View (The Primary Chords in a Key)

In Level 1, we learned about the theory behind the Power Chord, a chord that is made up of just 2 notes. A triad, however, is a 3 note chord. Before we get into the nitty gritty details of the triad, the best way to gain a solid foundation for how they work is to understand how you can use a scale to create each chord. We will explain triads using the key of C and key of G. There will be exercises in the key of D for you to do.

The reason why I notate each scale degree with a caret (aka circumflex) above each number is because this is how you will see them represented in AP Music Theory study guides and College-level textbooks. Step 2: Use Chord Degrees to Build Chords From Each Note In The Scale (stacking thirds)

Scales have scale degrees and chords have chord degrees. When building the chord off of the first scale degree, the scale degrees and chord degrees match up. Therefore it’s very easy to make and see this triad. The chord degrees are the small numbers (without carets) just to the left of each note in the staff.

The reason why chord degrees are different than scale degrees is because chord degrees can go above 8 (beyond the octave) while scale degrees do not. This is an important concept to understand when we get to complex jazz chords in Level 3.

The resulting chord built from the 1st scale degree gets the Roman Numeral “I” because its root note is the first scale degree. The Roman Numeral is placed above the chord. This chord is also called the tonic chord because its root note is the tonic note of the scale.

Next, here are the steps for building the “Four chord” for any key.

  1. The root of the chord starts on the 4th scale degree.
  2. Skip a note.
  3. The third of the chord is the 6th scale degree.
  4. Skip a note.
  5. The fifth of the chord is the 1st scale degree.

Now, build the Four chord in the key of D. Helpful hint: use the D that is on the B (2nd) string, 3rd fret as one of your chord tones.

The steps for building the Five chord are similar. Just begin on the 5th scale degree.

Finally, build the Five chord in the key of D. Helpful hint: you can use the open string E as part of the Five chord.

Summary

In any key, the One (I), Four (IV), and Five (V) chords are called the Primary Chords. Here are the Primary Chords in the keys of C, G, and D Major organized in a table format.
