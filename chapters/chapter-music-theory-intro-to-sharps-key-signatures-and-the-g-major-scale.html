Music Theory: Intro to Sharps, Naturals, Key Signatures, and the G Major Scale

So far, we’ve been learning and discussing the key of C—the C Major scale, Power Chords, and chord progressions in that key. The notes in the key of C are called “natural” notes. In reality, the full and complete name of each note is “C natural”, “D natural”, etc. However, most people will leave the “natural” part of the name off and just simply say “C”, “D”, etc.

The musical symbol for “natural” is ♮. So if we’re being unnecessarily pedantic the C Major scale would be labeled like this:

There are a few other ways to name a note. Some notes can get a “sharp”.

Let’s learn a new scale by building the G Major scale (a major scale where the tonic is G). Because all scales must contain each note name, we’ll first cut out a G string from all the available notes like so:

We now have all 12 notes from G to G. The second thing we need to check is to see if the labeled notes follow the 221-2221 Major Scale pattern.

They do not.

As you can see, the green F is not in the scale. Instead the unlabeled black note just before G should be in the scale as a labeled, “rainbow” note. What is the name of that note? The way that music theory solves this problem is by calling that unlabeled note “F♯” (pronounced “F sharp”). We still need to have all 7 letters represented in the scale. We also need to indicate that the note is not F♮(“natural”).

Now both major scale attributes are accounted for:

  1. The Major Scale must have each note name present. None are skipped and none are used twice. Even though the note, F, has a sharp symbol next to it, all 7 letter names are used (G, A, B, C, D, E, and F).
  2. The scale must follow the “221-2221” (or wwh-w-wwh) interval pattern.

Key Signature

A “key signature” tells the performer which notes become a sharp or flat. Music written in the key of C has no “accidentals” (sharps or flats) indicated. Here is a simply ascending melody in the key of C:

In standard notation the key signature is placed at the beginning of a piece or the beginning of a section. It is circled in red here:

Now this piece of music is in the key of G. Music written in the key of G will have one sharp—F sharp. In standard notation, only one sharp symbol is placed on the far left. The very top line represents the note F. However, all F’s will be sharped.

Notice that you’ll need to instead play the 4th fret of the D string (measure 1) and the 2nd fret of the D string (measure 3) when you see an “F” in standard notation (as it should be sharped).

  • The key signature for the key of G is: one sharp and that is F♯.
  • They key signature for the key of C is: no sharps and no flats.
