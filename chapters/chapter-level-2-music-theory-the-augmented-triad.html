Music Theory: The Augmented Triad

Chapter objective. Build, spell, recognize, and play an augmented triad without treating this less-common color as part of the ordinary Major or natural-minor chord field.

Retrieve first. Spell C Major and name the two 3rds inside it. Then raise only its 5th by one half step.

Review point. Compare Major, minor, diminished, and augmented formulas before the final Level 2 application.

You have spent most of Level 2 on the triads guitarists use every day: Major and minor, with diminished appearing naturally at the edge of a key. There is one more basic triad quality worth recognizing, even though you will meet it less often.

Raise the 5th

An augmented triad starts as a Major triad and raises the 5th by one half step—one fret on the guitar.

1 – 3 – ♯5

C Major is C–E–G. Raise G one half step to G♯ and you get C–E–G♯: C augmented.

ChordFormulaNotesSymbol
C Major1–3–5C–E–GC
C augmented1–3–♯5C–E–G♯Caug or C+

Two Major 3rds

A Major triad stacks a Major 3rd and then a minor 3rd. An augmented triad stacks two Major 3rds: C to E is four half steps, and E to G♯ is four more.

That equal spacing makes the chord symmetrical. Continue another four half steps above G♯ and you return to C in the next octave. On the fretboard, the chord divides the octave into three equal four-fret pieces.

The symmetry is why an augmented chord can feel as if it is leaning in several directions at once. At this level, you do not need a catalog of its possible resolutions. Hear it as a bright, tense, less-settled color and recognize the raised 5th.

Put Caug on the guitar

Play only the D, G, and B strings:

StringDGB
Fret1099
NoteCEG♯
Degree13♯5

Compare it with the nearby C Major triad on the same strings: D string 10, G string 9, B string 8. Move only the B-string note up one fret, from G to G♯. That single raised note turns C into Caug.

Not part of the ordinary chord field

Augmented triads do not occur diatonically in the Major scale or the natural-minor scale. That is one reason they are less common than Major and minor triads. Other forms of minor can produce one, but those scales belong to later material.

For now, do not force Caug into the C Major chord field or the D natural-minor chord field you already built. Treat it as a special color whose construction you can recognize: Major triad, raised 5th.

Hear it

  1. Play C Major on D–G–B at frets 10–9–8.
  2. Raise only the B-string note to the 9th fret and play Caug: 10–9–9.
  3. Return to C Major. Listen to the G♯ relax down to G.

Try it yourself

  1. Spell Gaug from the formula 1–3–♯5.
  2. Spell Daug.
  3. How many half steps separate each neighboring pair of chord tones in an augmented triad?
Answer key
  • Gaug: G–B–D♯.
  • Daug: D–F♯–A♯.
  • Four half steps: each interval is a Major 3rd.

Augmented is not an everyday home base. It is a specific color: 1–3–♯5, two Major 3rds, one raised note.
