Music Theory: Sus Chords

Chapter objective. Construct sus2 and sus4 chords by replacing—not merely omitting—the 3rd, and distinguish their note sets from Major and minor triads.

Retrieve first. Spell C and Cm. Keep the root and fifth fixed, then predict the notes of Csus2 and Csus4 before looking.

Review point. Resolve a sus4 to a Major chord on the barre shapes in Chapter 33, then use it as intentional harmonic decoration in the final assessment.

You now know how ordinary triads are built. Stack a 3rd on a root, stack another 3rd on top, and you get a chord with three jobs: root, 3rd, and 5th.

The 3rd has been the note with the opinion. It decides whether the chord sounds Major or minor. Move that one note and the whole color changes.

So what happens if you take the 3rd away?

You get a sus chord.

Sus = the 3rd is suspended

A sus chord is a chord that replaces the 3rd with the note a 2nd or 4th above its root.

That is the important word: replaces. A power chord leaves the 3rd out and gives you only 1 and 5. A sus chord does not leave an empty space. It puts another note where the 3rd normally lives.

There are two common versions:

  • sus4 = 1-4-5
  • sus2 = 1-2-5

Notice the language. The 2 and the 4 describe the chord from its root. In the examples below, which use the key of C, those notes are also the 2nd and 4th scale degrees, D and F. You do not need the formal interval names yet. For now, read the number as a place inside the chord, measured from its root.

Csus4 = C-F-G

Start with C: C-E-G. Count those notes as scale degrees in the key of C: C is 1, E is 3, and G is 5.

Now replace the 3rd with the 4th. In C, the 4th degree is F. So Csus4 is C-F-G.

Chord1middle note5
CCE (3)G
Csus4CF (4)G

Only the middle note changed. But the chord no longer says Major, because the E is gone. It no longer says minor either. It holds its breath.

Csus2 = C-D-G

Sus2 works the same way, but it replaces the 3rd with the 2nd degree.

Count in C: C (1), D (2), E (3), F (4), G (5). So Csus2 is C-D-G.

Chord1middle note5
CCE (3)G
Csus2CD (2)G

Again, the root and 5th stay put. The only question is what you put in the middle.

Sus is not add

The open, floating quality of a sus chord comes from removing the 3rd, the note that normally tells your ear Major or minor. An add2 or add9 chord does something different: it keeps the 3rd and adds another note.

ChordNotesWhat happened to the 3rd?
Csus2C–D–GE was replaced
Cadd2C–D–E–GE remains
Cadd9C–E–G–DE remains; D is normally voiced higher

An add chord therefore normally contains four different chord tones. On guitar you may double some of them, but the defining difference remains: sus removes the 3rd; add keeps it.

A sus chord has three notes, but it is not a triad

This is a slightly picky point, but it prevents confusion later.

A triad is not just any three-note chord. In this book, a triad is a chord built by stacking 3rds. Major, minor, and diminished triads all follow that rule. Sus chords break it on purpose, because they remove the 3rd from the stack.

So, yes, Csus4 has three notes: C, F, and G. But we will call it a sus chord, not a triad.

Sus chords can move or stay open

A common sound is sus4 resolving to Major. In C, that means Csus4 moving to C: C-F-G becomes C-E-G. The F drops one step to E, and the chord relaxes.

Count the degrees and you can see the release: 1-4-5 becomes 1-3-5. The suspended note, 4, falls back to the deciding note, 3.

Sus2 sounds more open and less demanding. Csus2, C-D-G, can move to C too, with D rising to E. But modern guitar music also lets sus2 and sus4 chords stand on their own. They commonly resolve, but they do not have to resolve. Without a 3rd, the chord can keep its open or ambiguous color for as long as the music needs it. The stricter historical rules behind a suspension can wait until a later level.

One pitch set, two roots

Gsus2 is G–A–D. Dsus4 is D–G–A. Those are the same three pitch classes, reordered. The symbol tells your ear and your band which note is being treated as the root: G gives 1–2–5, while D gives 1–4–5.

Do not describe this as an inversion of the same-root chord. An inversion keeps the chord root and changes only which chord tone is lowest. Here the root interpretation changes, so Gsus2 and Dsus4 are two names for the same pitch set in different harmonic contexts.

Hear it

At a piano, or on the guitar in the next chapter, play these three chords slowly:

  1. Csus4: C-F-G
  2. C: C-E-G
  3. Csus2: C-D-G

Listen to what disappeared. Neither sus chord has E, so neither one clearly says Major. The 3rd is the mood note, and sus chords replace it. The new note may resolve, or it may remain as the chord’s open color.

Try it yourself

Build the sus4 and sus2 chords below by replacing the 3rd. Use the Major scale degrees you already know.

  1. G is G-B-D. What is Gsus4?
  2. G is G-B-D. What is Gsus2?
  3. D is D-F♯-A. What is Dsus4?
Answer key
  • Gsus4 = G-C-D. Replace B (3) with C (4).
  • Gsus2 = G-A-D. Replace B (3) with A (2).
  • Dsus4 = D-G-A. Replace F♯ (3) with G (4).

That is the whole theory: sus4 is 1-4-5, sus2 is 1-2-5, and both replace the 3rd so the chord can resolve or keep hovering.
