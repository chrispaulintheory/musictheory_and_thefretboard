Level 3 · Chapter 4

Note Choice: Make Melody Connect to Chords

So far, we have listened to entrances, endings, duration, and silence. We have also listened to the paths phrases draw through pitch.

Now we will focus on note choice.

The same note can feel settled over one chord and restless over another. Harmony gives each melody note a role.

We can hear that relationship clearly by holding the chord still and changing only the melody note.

One Chord, Seven Colors

We will begin with a C chord and the seven notes of the C Major scale:

C   D   E   F   G   A   B

In C Major, those notes are scale degrees 1 through 7. Over the tonic C chord, each degree creates a different relationship with the harmony.

The descriptions below are listening handles rather than permanent definitions. Register, rhythm, duration, chord voicing, tone, and genre can change the effect of every note. You may eventually invent better descriptions of your own.

Degree Note over C Relationship A useful listening description
1 C Root Home. C creates the clearest arrival and often feels complete enough to end the melody.
2 D Major 2nd / 9th “Maybe?” D can sound open, spacious, or like a gentle question that has not been answered yet.
3 E Major 3rd Peaceful warmth. E reveals the chord’s major quality and feels stable, warm, and clear.
4 F Perfect 4th / 11th The annoying toddler on the plane. F rubs against E in the chord and insistently asks for something to happen, often a move down to E.
5 G Perfect 5th Powerful. G feels stable, open, and strong, like a support beam inside the chord.
6 A Major 6th / 13th Soaring. A can feel expansive, tender, or slightly wistful while remaining relatively smooth against the chord.
7 B Major 7th You really need to use the restroom. B sits one half step below home and creates urgency, delicacy, or an aching pull toward C.

Hear the Seven Degrees

To isolate each color, compare scale degrees 1 through 7 over the same sustained C chord. Keep the register, duration, tone, volume, and chord voicing identical.

Listen once without looking at the descriptions. Give each note your own word, image, or physical sensation. Then compare your reactions with the table.

On a second listen, notice three broad families:

  • 1, 3, and 5 form the structure of the chord.
  • 2 and 6 add relatively smooth color.
  • 4 and 7 create stronger friction or direction.

Which notes feel complete enough to end on? Which add color while remaining comfortable? Which make you expect something to happen next?

Pentatonic Scales as Color Filters

A pentatonic scale does more than reduce the number of available notes. It selects a particular combination of harmonic colors.

Compare these two familiar collections:

A minor pentatonic: A C D E G

E minor pentatonic: E G A B D

They share four notes: A, D, E, and G. One note changes. A minor pentatonic contains C, while E minor pentatonic contains B.

That one-note difference gives us an unusually clean listening experiment.

Over a C Chord

A Minor Pentatonic

Relative to C, A minor pentatonic gives us:

6   1   2   3   5

It contains all three notes of the C triad: C, E, and G. It also contains D and A, two relatively smooth colors.

A minor pentatonic and C major pentatonic contain the same notes. Over C, this collection can sound consonant, easy-going, and clearly connected to the chord. Depending on the phrasing, rhythm, and tone, it is common in country, folk, pop, and roots music.

E Minor Pentatonic

Relative to C, E minor pentatonic gives us:

3   5   6   7   2

It contains E and G from the chord, together with the 9th, 13th, and major 7th. The melody never plays C, so the accompaniment supplies the sense of home.

The B adds the major-7th color. That one note can make the collection sound more luminous, jazzy, or sophisticated than A minor pentatonic over the same chord.

A Controlled Comparison

Listen to two nearly identical phrases:

Version A: E G A G E D C

Version B: E G A G E D B

The rhythm, entrance, ending time, tone, and general contour remain the same. The final note changes from C to B.

  • How does the final C create arrival?
  • How does the final B keep the phrase open?
  • How much character changes when one note changes?

Over an Am Chord

A Minor Pentatonic

Relative to A, the notes are:

1   ♭3   4   5   ♭7

This collection contains the complete Am triad: A, C, and E. The C states the minor quality directly. The result is the familiar, grounded, and often predictable minor-pentatonic sound.

E Minor Pentatonic

Relative to A, the notes are:

5   ♭7   1   9   11

This collection contains A and E from the chord while leaving the minor 3rd, C, to the accompaniment. B functions as the 9th, and D functions as the 11th.

The result can feel more spacious, suspended, or modern. The 9th, 11th, and flat 7th become prominent colors while the harmony underneath continues to establish Am.

The two pentatonic collections still differ by only one note: C changes to B. The 4th and flat 7th appear in both collections; the fresh note is the 9th, and the defining minor 3rd disappears from the melody.

  • How strongly does C reveal the minor quality?
  • What happens when B replaces C?
  • Which version states the chord more directly?
  • Which version leaves more harmonic space?

The Harmony Changes the Meaning

The same C that sounded like home over C becomes the minor 3rd over Am.

The same B that sounded like a major 7th over C becomes the 9th over Am.

Theory gives the note a name. Harmony gives the note a role. The melody decides when that role will be heard.

Listen for Target Tones

A target tone is a note that the melody emphasizes through placement, duration, repetition, or arrival.

Chord tones usually create the most direct connection to the harmony. Other scale tones contribute different degrees of color, friction, and motion.

When you analyze a professional melody or solo, listen for the notes that receive emphasis. Then identify their relationship to the chord underneath them. That relationship is the heart of note choice.

Listening Assignment

Choose a short passage from a professional melody or solo and listen for the notes that sound emphasized.

  1. Notice notes that are long, repeated, accented, or placed at phrase endings.
  2. Describe each emphasized note with a word or image before naming it theoretically.
  3. Identify the chord underneath the note when you can.
  4. Describe the note as stable, colorful, tense, urgent, open, or resolved.
  5. Listen for repeated colors and the moments when the musician introduces a new one.

Chapter Summary

  • Every melody note creates a relationship with the chord underneath it.
  • Scale degrees can be learned as audible colors before they are treated as rules.
  • Different pentatonic collections act as different harmonic color filters.
  • A minor and E minor pentatonic differ by one note, yet that change can strongly alter the sound over C or Am.
  • Target tones reveal which note choices the melody wants the listener to notice.

You can now listen through all three components: phrasing, melodic shape, and note choice.
