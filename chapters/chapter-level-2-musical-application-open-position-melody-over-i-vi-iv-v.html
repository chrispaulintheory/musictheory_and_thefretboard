Musical Application: Open-Position Melody over I-vi-IV-V

Chapter objective. Create a short, rhythmic melody over a chord loop by targeting current chord tones at structurally important moments and using other scale notes as connection rather than filler.

Retrieve first. Spell C, Am, F, and G. For each chord, circle its root, 3rd, and 5th in the C Major scale before playing a melody note.

Assessment point. The guided C-Major loop below is a rehearsal. The Level 2 capstone at the end of this chapter requires a new tonal setting, multiple voicings, a written explanation, and a delayed transfer task.

Level 1 ended with a practical payoff: you used note memorization and power chords to play along with real songs.

Level 2 gets the same kind of payoff. This time the job is melody.

You now know the open-position C Major scale, the I–vi–IV–V progression, and the idea that one key gives you one palette. Put those together and you can improvise a simple melody over a chord loop without learning any new theory.

The loop

Use this progression in C:

C → Am → F → G

In Roman numerals, that is I–vi–IV–V. Every chord uses notes from the C Major palette, so your melody can use the C Major scale the whole time.

That is the most important practical rule in this chapter: do not switch scales when the chord changes. The chords are different brush-loads from the same palette.

The melody palette

Use the open-position C Major scale you learned earlier:

NoteCDEFGABC
String/fretA 3D 0D 2D 3G 0G 2B 0B 1

Do not use the whole scale at first. Use only three notes: C, D, and E. A small palette is easier to make musical than a large one.

First pass: rhythm before notes

Put on a slow loop, or record yourself strumming C, Am, F, and G with four even beats per chord.

Now play only C. Make it interesting with rhythm. Hold it for two beats. Repeat it four times. Leave a rest. Come in late. Your first job is not to find impressive notes. Your first job is to make one note sound intentional.

This is exactly like the Level 1 power-chord application. Simple material, played in time, sounds like music faster than complicated material played randomly.

Second pass: aim for chord tones

A chord tone is a note that belongs to the chord happening right now. You already know these:

ChordChord tones
CC-E-G
AmA-C-E
FF-A-C
GG-B-D

Notice that C belongs to three of the four chords: C, Am, and F. That makes it a friendly anchor note. E belongs to C and Am. G belongs to C and G.

So try this: when the chord changes, land on a note that belongs to the new chord. You are still using the same C Major scale. You are simply choosing a note that agrees with the chord underneath.

Third pass: question and answer

Make a two-bar question over C and Am. Then answer it over F and G.

For example, play C-D-E over C, then hold E over Am. That is your question. Then play F-E-D over F, and land on G over G. That is your answer.

The exact notes matter less than the shape. A melody usually feels better when it breathes in small sentences instead of running continuously.

A simple practice routine

  1. Strum or loop C → Am → F → G.
  2. Play only C for one full loop, changing rhythm.
  3. Add D and E for the next loop.
  4. Add the rest of the open-position C Major scale.
  5. On each chord change, try to land on a chord tone.

If you get lost, shrink the palette again. Go back to C, D, and E. The goal is not to use all seven notes. The goal is to make the notes you choose sound connected to the chords.

Hear it

Record the chord loop for one minute. Then play one-note melodies over it. Seriously: one note. Change only the rhythm and the spaces between attacks.

When that starts to feel boring, add one neighboring note. That is how improvisation grows: not by dumping more notes into your hand, but by making each new note earn its place.

Track your progress

Add this to your Progress Tracker: improvise over C → Am → F → G for one minute using the open-position C Major scale, without stopping when you make a mistake. Keep time first. Correct notes come second.

Try it yourself

Write a four-measure melody, one measure per chord. Use only quarter notes and half notes. Circle every note that is a chord tone for the chord underneath it.

Success standard. Verify every circled note by spelling the chord underneath it. Then perform the four measures twice at 60 bpm over a recorded loop. Pass when the rhythm continues without a restart and the first note of every measure is a chord tone of the current chord.

That final step is an important rehearsal: your ear, your chord knowledge, and your fretboard map are now part of the same practice loop.

Level 2 capstone: design, perform, explain

The C-Major loop above is a guided model. This capstone checks whether the underlying ideas transfer when the key, tonic, voicing, and melodic choices are no longer supplied. Work at a slow, steady tempo; 50–60 bpm is appropriate. Record the result or perform it for a teacher or peer so that timing and sound can be evaluated rather than assumed.

Use the four-part rubric at the end to score the finished result. Do not count the project complete because it has been recorded; each rubric area must meet its stated standard.

Part 1: make a 16-bar harmonic plan

Use the F-Major/D-natural-minor relative pair. Write the chord symbol, Roman numeral, and three chord tones for every bar before you play it.

  1. Bars 1–8, F Major: play I–V–vi–IV twice: F → C → Dm → B♭. In either C bar, use Csus4 for two beats, then resolve it to C for two beats.
  2. Bars 9–16, D natural minor: play i–VI–VII–i twice: Dm → B♭ → C → Dm.

The required pair forces you to use the B♭ introduced in the final unit and to demonstrate that the same note palette can support two different tonal centers.

Part 2: make the harmony musical

Across the 16 bars, include all of the following. Plan them first; do not add them only if they happen by accident.

  • At least one full E-shape or A-shape barre chord and at least two compact triad voicings.
  • Two different inversions of the same triad, with their bass notes named in your written plan.
  • One short connective texture: either a Major/minor 6th or a spread-triad figure.
  • The Csus4-to-C resolution in the F-Major section, with the moving note named.

Part 3: add a melody that follows the harmony

Write or improvise a melody over all 16 bars using only F Major/D natural minor notes. On the first beat of every chord change, land on that chord's root, 3rd, or 5th. Use at least two non-chord tones as passing or neighboring notes between those targets. Mark the chord-tone targets on your written plan.

Part 4: explain what you made

With the recording or performance, submit a short written or spoken explanation that answers all four questions.

  1. What notes belong to both F Major and D natural minor, and what makes F feel like home in the first section and D feel like home in the second?
  2. What are the Roman numerals and broad functions of F, C, Dm, and B♭ in the F-Major section?
  3. Spell one Major triad and one minor triad from your piece. Identify the 3rd that gives each its quality.
  4. Name one inversion, 6th, or 10th/spread voicing you used, and explain what changed: bass note, spacing, or both.

Delayed transfer check

On the following day, without reopening this capstone, choose G or A. Write and play I–IV–V–I with the E-shape/A-shape system, then add one sus4-to-Major resolution on V. Name the roots, Roman numerals, and resolving note. This delayed attempt measures whether the system survived beyond the rehearsed F/D-minor project.

Capstone rubric
  • Harmony and theory: chord spellings, numerals, tonal centers, and the sus resolution are correct.
  • Fretboard control: chosen voicings contain the intended chord tones; inversions and interval textures are named, not guessed.
  • Musicianship: pulse continues through chord changes and melody targets chord tones on each change.
  • Transfer: the next-day I–IV–V task works in G or A without copying a prepared example.

Level 2 is complete only when all four areas are secure. If one area is weak, return to its named chapter, practise one smaller task, and repeat the capstone after a delay rather than immediately repeating the same take.
