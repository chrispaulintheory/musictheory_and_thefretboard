Building a Melody Over I-vi-IV-V

Now we put the three parts together.

Phrasing.

Melodic shape.

Note choice.

The progression is the one you already know:

C -> Am -> F -> G

In Roman numerals:

I-vi-IV-V

The goal is a four-bar melody, one bar per chord.

We will build it in stages.

Stage 1: choose the important notes

Start with the landing notes.

That may feel backwards. Most people start by noodling around until something sounds good. Sometimes that works. But it is hard to repeat, and it is hard to fix.

So aim first.

Choose one chord tone for each chord:

C: E

Am: C

F: A

G: G

Now write the skeleton:

E | C | A | G

One note per bar.

This is not the melody yet. It is the frame.

Stage 2: draw the melodic shape

Now look at the important notes as a shape.

E down to C.

C down to A.

A down to G.

That is mostly descending.

Descending motion can sound settled, which is useful. But if the whole melody only falls, it may run out of energy.

So let's give it a small arch inside the first two bars:

C D E | E D C | C B A | A G

The important note choices are still there:

  • E over C,
  • C over Am,
  • A over F,
  • G over G.

But now the melody has a path between them.

Stage 3: add phrasing

Right now, the line is still too even.

Even notes are useful for practice, but melodies need breath.

So add a rest and one repeated phrase rhythm:

C D E - | E D C - | C B A - | A - G -

The first three bars now share the same rhythm. The last bar changes slightly so the phrase feels like it is ending.

That is not an accident.

Repetition creates identity. A small change creates arrival.

Stage 4: revise the high point

Every melody has a highest note.

Sometimes the highest note is just wherever the hand happened to go. But it is better when the high point has a reason.

In our line, the highest note is E:

C D E

That happens right away, in bar 1. That can work, but it makes the phrase spend its energy early.

Try moving the high point later:

C D E - | E F E C | C B A - | A - G -

Now F is the high point, and it happens over the Am bar as a scale tone. It does not land as the target; it passes through and returns to C.

That gives the phrase a little lift before it settles.

Stage 5: choose the ending

The final note matters.

If the phrase ends on G over the G chord, it sounds open. G is the root of the G chord, and the progression wants to loop back to C.

If the phrase ends on C, it sounds more resolved, even though the chord underneath is G. That C points back home.

Try both endings:

Open ending:

A - G -

Homeward ending:

A G C -

Neither is wrong. They set up different next moves.

If the loop is going to repeat, the open ending may be better. If the phrase is finished, the homeward ending may be better.

The full draft melody

Here is one version:

Bar 1, C: C D E -

Bar 2, Am: E F E C

Bar 3, F: C B A -

Bar 4, G: A G C -

Now check the three parts.

Phrasing: the first and third bars share a three-note idea plus space.

Melodic shape: the line rises, reaches F, then comes down.

Note choice: E belongs to C, C belongs to Am, A belongs to F, and the ending C points home through the G chord.

That is a melody draft.

Not a perfect melody. A draft.

And because you built it in layers, you can revise it.

Hear it

Loop C -> Am -> F -> G.

Play only the skeleton first:

E | C | A | G

Then play the connected version:

C D E | E D C | C B A | A G

Then play the rhythmized version:

C D E - | E F E C | C B A - | A G C -

Listen to what each layer adds.

The note choices connect the line to the chords.

The melodic shape gives it direction.

The phrasing makes it speak.

Try it yourself

Build your own four-bar melody over C -> Am -> F -> G.

Do it in four passes:

  1. Choose one important note per chord.
  2. Connect those notes with nearby scale tones.
  3. Add at least two rests.
  4. Decide where the highest note happens.

When you are done, circle the important note choices and mark the highest note.

If the melody sounds weak, do not throw it away. Fix one layer.

Figure needed

Create a four-stage build figure:

  1. note-choice skeleton,
  2. connected melodic shape,
  3. rhythmized phrase,
  4. revised final melody.

The caption should say: write the landings first, draw the path second, then use phrasing to make the line speak.
