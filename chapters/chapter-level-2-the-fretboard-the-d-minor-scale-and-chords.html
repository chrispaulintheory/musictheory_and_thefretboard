The Fretboard: The D Minor Scale and Chords

Chapter objective. Play and spell D natural minor in open position, build all seven of its diatonic triads, and hear why its natural-minor v has a gentler return to i than Major-key V has to I.

Retrieve first. From memory, write D natural minor and circle D, F, and A. Then spell Gm and Am using only that palette.

Review point. The same note collection is re-heard with F as tonic in Chapters 36–37; test that change of home in the Unit 7 checkpoint.

The D minor scale is D, E, F, G, A, B♭, C, D.

D minor in open position

Here is one clean octave of D minor, starting on the open D string and ending on the B string:

Degree12345671
NoteDEFGAB♭CD
String/fretD 0D 2D 3G 0G 2G 3B 1B 3

Play it slowly: open, 2, 3 on the D string; open, 2, 3 on the G string; 1, 3 on the B string.

Notice how friendly the shape is. The new note, B♭, lands at the 3rd fret of the G string. One flat, one very playable spot.

Compare it to D Major

Here is the ear test. Play the D Major scale, then the D minor scale, both starting on D.

D MajorDEF♯GABC♯D
D minorDEFGAB♭CD

The tonic did not move. D is still home. But three colors dropped: 3, 6, and 7. That is the sound of parallel Major and minor.

The home chord: Dm

The home chord in D minor is Dm: D, F, A. You already know this shape from the minor-triad chapters. In open position it is also one of the first guitar chords most players learn.

Listen to how the scale and chord agree. The scale's 1, 3, and 5 are D, F, and A. Those are exactly the notes of Dm. The home chord uses the home scale degrees.

Build the whole natural-minor chord field

The same method builds a triad on every note of D natural minor. Start on a scale note, take that note, skip one, take one, skip one, and take one more. When you reach the end, wrap around to D and keep counting. You are stacking alternate notes of one seven-note palette.

The quality pattern is worth learning as one line: minor, diminished, Major, minor, minor, Major, Major—or i–ii°–III–iv–v–VI–VII.

Notice the III chord: F–A–C spells F. The key of F Major is also the relative major of D minor. Both keys use the same seven notes; which note and chord sound like home tells your ear which key you are in.

This table stays strictly inside natural minor. That is why v is Am, not A: C remains natural, with no raised leading tone. Harmonic and melodic minor change the palette and some of its chords, but both belong to later material.

Two useful natural-minor progressions

Minor keys use Roman numerals too. We write the home chord with a lowercase i, because the tonic chord is minor.

Here are two useful D minor progressions:

NumeralsiVIVIIi
In D minorDmB♭CDm
Numeralsiivvi
In D minorDmGmAmDm

The first one, i-VI-VII-i, is a classic rock and folk natural-minor sound. The second one, i-iv-v-i, is a natural-minor loop: home, away, a gentler turn through v, then home again. Unlike Major-key V, the natural-minor v chord has no raised leading tone, so its pull back to i is softer.

The B♭ problem

B♭ is the hardest chord here because it sits at the 1st fret as an A-shape barre. That is a tough spot on the neck. The strings are stiff, and the fret is wide.

So use the practical guitar solution: if the 1st-fret A-shape B♭ is too hard right now, play B♭ as an E-shape barre at the 6th fret. Same chord, different neighborhood.

This is not cheating. It is exactly why you learned two movable barre shapes.

Hear it

Play Dm → B♭ → C → Dm slowly. Then sing the scale over it, using only the open-position D minor notes above. The B♭ chord will sound like the new color in the room, because it is the first flat doing real musical work.

Track your progress

Add this to your Progress Tracker: D minor in open position, ascending and descending, as quarter notes at 80 BPM without errors. Say the note names first. Then say the degrees: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 1.

Try it yourself

Use the D minor scale to answer these, then transfer the pattern.

  1. What are the notes of the i chord?
  2. What are the notes of the iv chord?
  3. What are the notes of the v chord?
  4. Choose another natural-minor key. Write its seven scale notes, stack alternate notes from every degree, and label the results i–ii°–III–iv–v–VI–VII.
Answer key
  • i = Dm: D-F-A.
  • iv = Gm: G-B♭-D.
  • v = Am: A-C-E.
  • Transfer check: your chord qualities should be minor, diminished, Major, minor, minor, Major, Major. For A natural minor, the chords are Am, B°, C, Dm, Em, F, and G.

The palette named the available notes, and the chords gave those notes a place to live. That is the Level 2 loop: palette, chord tones, fretboard, sound.
