Music Theory: The Major Triad

Chapter objective. Construct and identify a Major triad as root, Major 3rd, and Perfect 5th; explain why the 3rd, rather than the outer fifth, supplies its quality.

Retrieve first. Write C-E-G from memory. Label the C-E and E-G intervals before checking this chapter.

Review point. You will turn this structure into a fretboard shape in Chapter 7, contrast it with the minor triad in Chapter 8, revisit voicings in Chapter 10, and use the full unit in the Chapter 11 assessment.

In the previous chapter, we quickly built out the 7 triads in a Major key (the key of C Major) using a simple 1-3-5 formula where each note of the scale became the root note of the chord to be built. Here is what we built:

Chord Degree
⬇
5 G A B C D E F
3 E F G A B C D
1 C D E F G A B
Roman Numeral ⮕ I ii iii IV V vi vii°

Now, focus on the foundational 3rd interval (from the root to the 3rd). Here is the table from "Chapter 3: Harmonizing the C Major Scale in 3rds" that shows this:

3 ➡ E F G A B C D
1 ➡ C (1̂) D (2̂) E (3̂) F (4̂) G (5̂) A (6̂) B (7̂)
M3 = Major 3rd,
m3 = minor 3rd ➡		 M3 m3 m3 M3 M3 m3 m3

Look again at the interval from the 1st chord degree to the 3rd. Three pairs in the C Major scale—C–E, F–A, and G–B—are Major 3rds. Those pairs form the foundations of the I, IV, and V chords.

A Major 3rd on the bottom is only half of the story. Each chord needs one more note, a minor 3rd above the 3rd:

  • E–G completes C
  • A–C completes F
  • B–D completes G

The figure below places all three chords on one horizontal natural-note ruler. The black dots mark the chromatic notes between the labeled natural notes. Use the walkthroughs below to trace each chord's lane in four passes: first the Major 3rd, then the minor 3rd, then all three chord degrees, and finally the interval between the outer notes.

Build C: C–E–G

The chord symbol C is pronounced “C Major.” A chord symbol written as a letter by itself names a Major chord.

  1. Build the bottom 3rd: C up to E is 4 half steps, a Major 3rd (M3).
  2. Stack the top 3rd: E up to G is 3 half steps, a minor 3rd (m3).
  3. Label the triad: C is the root (1), E is the 3rd (3), and G is the 5th (5).
  4. Check the outer notes: C up to G is 7 half steps, a Perfect 5th (P5).

Build F: F–A–C

  1. Build the bottom 3rd: F up to A is 4 half steps, a Major 3rd (M3).
  2. Stack the top 3rd: A up to the next C is 3 half steps, a minor 3rd (m3).
  3. Label the triad: F is the root (1), A is the 3rd (3), and C is the 5th (5).
  4. Check the outer notes: F up to C is 7 half steps, a Perfect 5th (P5).

Build G: G–B–D

  1. Build the bottom 3rd: G up to B is 4 half steps, a Major 3rd (M3).
  2. Stack the top 3rd: B up to the next D is 3 half steps, a minor 3rd (m3).
  3. Label the triad: G is the root (1), B is the 3rd (3), and D is the 5th (5).
  4. Check the outer notes: G up to D is 7 half steps, a Perfect 5th (P5).

The Major triad formula

A Major triad is defined by two stacked 3rds. Starting from the root, a Major 3rd reaches the 3rd of the chord. A minor 3rd stacked above it reaches the 5th. Therefore, M3 + m3 is the interval formula for a Major triad.

Chord 1 3 5 Stacked 3rds Outer interval
C (I) C E G M3 + m3 P5
F (IV) F A C M3 + m3 P5
G (V) G B D M3 + m3 P5

Although each chord contains one Major 3rd and one minor 3rd, it is a Major triad because the foundational interval from 1 to 3 is Major. The 3rd supplies the chord's quality.

The outer notes make a Perfect 5th

Add the two stacked 3rds together and something familiar appears: 4 half steps + 3 half steps = 7 half steps. Seven half steps from the root to the 5th is a Perfect 5th—the same interval used to build the power chords from Level 1.

The Perfect 5th supplies a strong outer frame, but it does not tell you whether the triad is Major or minor. For that, you must look at the 3rd.

Hear it

Use the same order as the figure to hear each chord develop. On an instrument, sound the notes together; with your voice, sing them one after another.

  1. Sound the root and 3rd together. Listen for the Major 3rd.
  2. Sound the 3rd and 5th together. Listen for the minor 3rd.
  3. Sound 1, 3, and 5 together. Listen for the complete Major triad.
  4. Sound only 1 and 5. Notice the familiar Perfect 5th frame, then add the 3rd back to restore the chord's Major quality.

Repeat the sequence with C–E–G, F–A–C, and G–B–D. Three different roots produce the same bright, full, and settled character because all three chords share the M3 + m3 structure.

Try it yourself

Without looking at the figure, write the three notes of each chord: C, F, and G. Label each note 1, 3, or 5, then name the bottom 3rd, the top 3rd, and the interval between the outer notes.

Answer key
  • C: C (1), E (3), G (5). C–E is an M3, E–G is an m3, and C–G is a P5.
  • F: F (1), A (3), C (5). F–A is an M3, A–C is an m3, and F–C is a P5.
  • G: G (1), B (3), D (5). G–B is an M3, B–D is an m3, and G–D is a P5.

To sum the whole chapter up in one line: a Major triad is 1–3–5, built as a Major 3rd with a minor 3rd stacked on top, while its outer notes form a Perfect 5th.

In the next chapter, you will transfer this same M3 + m3 structure from the horizontal note ruler to three adjacent strings and turn it into movable Major-triad shapes on the fretboard.
