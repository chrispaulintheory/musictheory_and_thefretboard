Music Theory: Common Chord Progressions in a Key

Chapter objective. Recognize, build, and play common Major-key progression patterns in more than one key, explaining their broad function and choosing chord tones for structurally important melody notes.

Retrieve first. In C Major, write I, IV, V, and vi as chord symbols and spell their notes. Mark which notes are chord tones of G, the V chord.

Review point. Roman numerals become movable guitar choices in Chapter 31; progressions and their melodic targets are assessed in Chapter 33 and the capstone.

You now own the whole key. "The Rest of the Chords in the Key" gave every one of C's seven chords a symbol you can read on a chart (C, Dm, Em, F, G, Am, and B°) and a Roman numeral that names its job (I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, and vii°). The last chapter sorted those chords into families: tonic, subdominant, and dominant.

That is the complete palette and the basic map. But a palette is not yet a painting. A list of seven chords, however well labeled, is not music until the chords start to move. This chapter is about that movement: which chords tend to follow which, why a few short orders of chords turn up in song after song, and what it really means to play "in a key."

The tool that makes this chapter possible is the Roman numeral. Because the numerals name the pattern and not the specific chords, everything you learn here is true in every Major key at once. Learn that I–IV–V is a satisfying trip out and back, and you have learned it for C (C–F–G), for G (G–C–D), and for D (D–G–A) in the same breath.

I–IV–V: the three chords that hold up the key

Back in "Chords in a Key" you met the three primary triads, the chords built on the 1st, 4th, and 5th degrees: I, IV, and V. They are the core chords of a Major key. An enormous amount of music, folk, blues, country, rock and roll, and simple pop, is built from those three chords and nothing else. In C they are C, F, and G.

Why do just three chords cover so much ground? Between them they hold all seven colors of the palette. I is C–E–G, IV is F–A–C, and V is G–B–D. Together they cover C, D, E, F, G, A, and B: the whole C Major scale. That makes I, IV, and V a flexible starting palette, but pitch collection alone does not decide the right chord at a particular moment. Rhythm, bass movement, and the melody notes on strong beats matter. Start by matching a strong-beat melody note to a chord tone; use other scale notes as passing or neighboring motion.

Written as a progression, the classic order is I–IV–V, and then back home to I. In C:

Numeral ➡ I IV V I
Key of C ➡ C F G C
Key of G ➡ G C D G

Play it slowly and you can hear the shape of the trip: I is home, IV is a step away from home, V leans hard, and the return to I feels like arriving home again.

The 12-bar blues: I–IV–V, counted out

You have already played this progression, though you may not have known its name. In Level 1 you strummed power chords along with songs; a famous frame those songs use is the 12-bar blues, and it is nothing but I, IV, and V arranged over twelve measures. Now that you have triads, you can play the very same form with fuller chords. Here it is in C, one chord per measure:

1 2 3 4
C (I) C (I) C (I) C (I)
F (IV) F (IV) C (I) C (I)
G (V) F (IV) C (I) G (V)

Read left to right, top to bottom: four bars of I, two of IV, two of I, then one bar each of V, IV, I, and a final V that points back to the top. Three chords, twelve bars, and a form you will recognize in many songs.

The dominant and the leading tone: why V pulls so hard

The V chord has a special name and a special job. Musicians call the chord built on the 5th degree the dominant. In the key of C, G is the dominant. Of all seven chords, it is the one that most wants to move, and where it wants to move is home, to I.

The reason is hiding inside the chord. Look at the notes of G in the key of C: G, B, and D. That middle note, B, is the 7th degree of the C scale, the note you learned to sing as Ti. Count the distance from B up to home, C: B (0), C (1). One half step, the smallest move there is. A note sitting one half step below the tonic pulls upward so hard that it earns its own title: the leading tone. It leads. Sing "Ti" and try to stop there. Your ear will not let you; it wants to climb the last half step to "Do."

So the dominant chord carries the leading tone inside it. When V moves to I, that B slides up to C and the tension releases. That single half-step resolution, Ti to Do, tucked inside the change from G to C, is the sound of a key arriving home. It is why V–I feels less like a choice and more like a conclusion.

The four-chord loop: I–V–vi–IV

Add one minor chord to the primary triads and you get one of the most common progressions in modern pop: I–V–vi–IV. In C that is C–G–Am–F. It loops, four bars around and back to the top, for the length of an entire song. Once you know it you will hear it everywhere.

The new chord is vi, the minor triad on the 6th degree (Am in C). Its arrival is the whole trick: after the bright I and the leaning V, the loop lands on a minor chord, and that small turn toward darkness is what gives the progression its bittersweet, endlessly-singable feeling. A related version simply starts the same loop on the minor chord, vi–IV–I–V (Am–F–C–G in C), which sounds moodier because it opens on the minor chord before resolving toward Major.

Numeral ➡ I V vi IV
Key of C ➡ C G Am F
Key of G ➡ G D Em C

ii–V–I: the smoothest way home

One more short order of chords is worth knowing by name, because it is a backbone of jazz and turns up in many other styles too: ii–V–I. In C that is Dm–G–C. You could not have written it before this Level, because it needs the 2 chord, the minor triad on the 2nd degree, which you only recently gave a symbol.

It works because it is a chain of falling motion into the dominant. The ii chord sets up the V, and the V, carrying its leading tone, delivers you to I. Where I–IV–V walks out and back, ii–V–I is a short, smooth route straight home. Later, when you add 7ths to these chords in Level 3, ii–V–I becomes one of the most important progressions in the jazz vocabulary; the triad version you are playing now is its basic form.

One key, one palette

Here is a question almost every guitarist asks the first time they play C–F–G–C and then try to solo over it: "When the F chord comes around, do I switch to the F Major scale? And back to C Major when the C returns?" It feels reasonable. It is also one of the most common misunderstandings in beginning harmony, and you already have everything you need to see why it is wrong.

Go back to the very first idea in Level 1: the Major scale creates a palette of seven colors. The key is that palette. And each chord in the key is not a new palette, it is a brush loaded with three colors picked from the same one. When F arrives in the key of C, its notes are F, A, and C, and every one of those is already a color on the C Major palette. You did not switch palettes. You never left C. You just dipped your brush in three of its colors instead of a different three.

That is what "playing in a key" means: one palette for the whole song. When you solo over C–F–G–C, you draw the whole solo from the C Major scale, start to finish. The chords change underneath your melody, but the palette does not, and Do stays home the entire time. Changing chords is not changing keys, and it is not changing scales.

Why the same chord can belong to many keys

The misunderstanding comes from imagining that each chord carries its own scale around with it. But a chord does not work that way. A chord is built from intervals, a root with a stack of 3rds on top, and that construction knows nothing about any key. It is a key that reaches out and claims a chord, by noticing that the chord's notes all fit its palette.

Which means one chord can be claimed by several keys at once. Take the G chord (G, B, D):

  • In the key of C, G is the V chord (the dominant).
  • In the key of G, that very same chord is I, home itself.
  • In the key of D, it is the IV chord.

Same three notes, three different jobs, depending on which key is doing the claiming. Or take Em (E, G, B): it is iii in C, vi in G, and ii in D. If a chord truly carried its own scale, none of this could happen; a chord would mean one thing forever. Instead, the same chord can belong to several keys, which is exactly why a chord symbol written by itself, an F on a page, can never tell you what key the song is in. Only the surrounding music can.

So the brush metaphor holds all the way down: the chords name which three colors you are using at a given moment, and the key names the whole palette those colors were drawn from. Learn to hear the difference and the fretboard starts feeling like one home base you are moving around inside.

Hear it

Play C–G–Am–F slowly, four even strums per chord, and loop it a dozen times. Do not try to name anything; just let the four-chord turn settle into your ear until you could hum the bass line. Then put on a few songs you like and see how many ride that same loop. Recognizing a progression by sound, before you know its chords, is the ear-training payoff of this whole chapter.

Then do the smaller experiment that proves the "one palette" point. Hold a C chord, and hum Do. Change to F, and hum Do again, the same pitch. It still sounds like home, even though the chord under it moved. The chords travel; Do stays put. That is a key.

Try it yourself

You know three Major keys well: C, G, and D. Write out each of these progressions in all three keys, using chord symbols. Everything you need is the harmonized-key table from "The Rest of the Chords in the Key."

  1. I–IV–V–I
  2. I–V–vi–IV (the four-chord loop)
  3. ii–V–I
Answer key
  • I–IV–V–I — Key of C: C–F–G–C  ·  Key of G: G–C–D–G  ·  Key of D: D–G–A–D
  • I–V–vi–IV — Key of C: C–G–Am–F  ·  Key of G: G–D–Em–C  ·  Key of D: D–A–Bm–G
  • ii–V–I — Key of C: Dm–G–C  ·  Key of G: Am–D–G  ·  Key of D: Em–A–D

Look down the columns. The numerals never changed, only the letters did, because a progression written in Roman numerals is one idea that every Major key spells in its own notes. In the next chapter you will discover that on the guitar it is even better than that: because your chord shapes are movable, a progression written in numerals is not just one idea, it is one shape pattern you can slide into any key you like.
