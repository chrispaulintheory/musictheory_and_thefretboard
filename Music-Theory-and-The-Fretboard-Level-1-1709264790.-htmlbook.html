Hello! My name is Denisse Vallecillos and I’m a teacher, conductor, and professional violinist. I have a Bachelor’s Degree in Music Education and a Master’s Degree in Orchestral Conducting. I teach and direct orchestra ensembles at all levels. However, for 5 years and with the help from a local guitar teacher (Chris Paul), I taught a High School-level introductory guitar class.

When I started the class, I did a lot of research on pedagogy and guitar methods. The recommended learning path as outlined by the National Association for Music Education is one that is based on classical music and performing as an ensemble. I was already directing 5 orchestras and just did not have the time to take on yet another performing ensemble. Additionally, the guitar class was unofficially the pre-requisite for the AP Music Theory class if students wanted to learn some foundational concepts first. Therefore I decided that the class would focus on popular music. After about 8 to 12 weeks (depending on the size of the class), we were strumming along to Hotel California. It was a very popular class because we had fun! And yes, we still learned how to read standard notation (sheet music). If you purchase the ebook, one of the bonus sections will teach you how (and why) you should learn this valuable skill.

Anyway, this series of books (Music Theory and The Fretboard) simply contain my theory lesson plans that I taught to my high school students. And it’s because I found so much success with my classes that I felt like other people would like the way that Chris and I explain these theory concepts.

Who are you? Who this book is for…

This book is for the guitarist who has heard a lot about the importance of learning theory but does not know where to start. Ideally, you can play several open position chords (“cowboy chords”), play the rhythm part to some songs, and play a few riffs and scales. However, you don’t really know the fretboard that well and instead you rely on shapes.

But you want a better understanding of what exactly it is that you’re playing.

The result this book can help you achieve

My goal is to help the student create a strong foundation first. One of the things I noticed that most guitar students lack is a clear understanding of harmony. According to Aaron Copland’s “What to Listen for in Music” (first published in 1939), the four elements of music are rhythm, melody, harmony, and tone color (timbre). Much of music theory education is the study of harmony because of how complicated it can get. However, you don’t need to know everything about music theory in order to start applying it in real life.

The second thing I have discovered about the guitar world is that many educational resources teach you just the shortcuts, patterns, and shapes. There’s nothing wrong with shortcuts, patterns, and shapes. However, I view music theory as “the product”. While guitar-specific systems like CAGED are “the packaging”. An ideal learning pathway is one that has both—the fundamental music concepts and the shortcuts/patterns/shapes.

I have found a way to simplify the music theory learning pathway that guarantees a true understanding of how music works.

Ultimately, the result you will attain will be a confidence that comes from a solid foundation of competence in harmonic knowledge and fretboard mastery.

How and why my roadmap works

There’s no new information in this book that you can’t find elsewhere. However, I’ve carefully put together the information in a specific order to maximize efficiency and minimize overwhelm.

It’s very easy to write a boring theory book that covers an overwhelming set of concepts. It seems some authors are afraid of being criticized for leaving some stuff out.

The secret to finding rapid success is to focus on the most relevant information that will give you the most results possible. I have found that many well-meaning guitar teachers cannot resist the urge to include less-useful concepts (for beginners), like modes, in their introductory-level books. I purposefully exclude some concepts because the truth is that you can sound pretty good on the guitar when you get really good at the fundamentals. After you have mastered the fundamentals, you’ll be better equipped to learning more complex concepts.

This Level 1 book covers 2-note chords (power chords) and the bass strings (E, A, D). The Level 2 book will cover 3-note chords (triads) across all 6 strings. And the Level 3 book will cover 4-note chords (6ths and 7ths).

This book takes the abstract (theory) and the concrete (shapes) and puts them side-by-side. Look through the book; you’ll notice that each theory concept has a dedicated “theory” chapter and a dedicated “fretboard” chapter. I plan to keep the “theory” chapters light on words. If I can succinctly define each concept, then you can spend more time on the “fretboard” chapters studying the shapes.

Before we start, here is the whole map on one page. Aaron Copland (whom I mentioned a moment ago) named four elements of music: rhythm, melody, harmony, and tone color (timbre). This book focuses on these three elements:

Harmony, Melody, and Rhythm.

Everything in this book serves one of those three. And each pillar breaks down into three simple parts:

Harmony — notes played together (the chords under a song)

  • Chord construction — what notes make a chord. We start with the simplest one possible: the power chord (just two notes!). In level 2, we’ll tackle three note chords (triads, etc).
  • Chords in a key — which chords naturally “go together”.
  • Chords from two keys — how to sound like Nirvana and other great bands.

Melody — notes played in sequence (the part you sing)

  • Phrasing — where a musical line breathes; where it begins and ends.
  • Melodic shape — the contour of a line, its rise and fall.
  • Note choice — which notes you pick against the underlying harmony.

Rhythm — where every note sits in time (the pillar the other two stand on)

  • Beat & tempo — the pulse, and how fast it goes.
  • Note duration — how long each note is held; long notes versus short ones.
  • Meter — how beats group together (time signatures, and counting along).

Why we start with power chords

Most theory books begin harmony with the triad (a three-note chord). We start one rung lower, with the power chord because it’s a very common chord and there are only two notes to worry about. It is the simplest possible chord, and you can play real songs with it almost immediately.

Keep these three pillars in the back of your mind as you read. Every chapter is a tool for, or a piece of, Harmony, Melody, or Rhythm.

Music Theory: Pitch

1

“Pitch is the quality that makes it possible to judge sounds as ‘higher’ and ‘lower’ in the sense associated with musical melodies.” https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_(music)

You can feel this phenomenon in your throat when you sing along with songs. As you sing higher pitches, the larynx will rise in the vocal tract.

As you sing lower pitches, it will move downward.

Every sound produced by a musical instrument (including the human voice) vibrates at a particular rate, called its “fundamental frequency”. Pitch is how we hear that frequency: the faster the vibration, the higher the pitch. Frequencies can be represented visually as “waves”. An excellent website that you can use to see frequencies visually represented is https://academo.org/demos/wave-interference-beat-frequency.

Although the waves are not moving on that website, try to imagine them going from left to right. “Frequency” is the number of waves that pass by in a second.

Here are two waves. The unit of measurement is called “Hertz” which is abbreviated as “Hz”. The top wave’s frequency is 329.6 Hz. The bottom wave’s frequency is 164.8 Hz (exactly half of 329.6 Hz). Because the 2nd frequency is exactly half of the first, its wavelength is exactly twice as long.

I recommend that you switch on the sound to hear what these frequencies sound like on that website. You’ll notice that a frequency that is exactly twice (or half) as much as the other will sound like it “just fits” with the other frequency.

Here is another example of two frequencies that “just fit”. The top frequency is still 329.6 Hz. The bottom one is a one-fourth of that, 82.4 Hz.

These two frequencies sound like they "just fit" as well because the top frequency is a multiple of 2 (4x) of the bottom one.

Guitar application

This particular pair is worth noticing on guitar: 82.4 Hz is the pitch of the thick, low E string, and 329.6 Hz is the pitch of the thin, high E string. They are two octaves apart, so their fundamental frequencies are exact multiples of each other.

Bass application

On a four-string bass, the open E string is about 41.2 Hz. At the 12th fret it produces an E at about 82.4 Hz—exactly twice the frequency. Both pitches are named E; the 12th-fret E is a lighter shade of the open E.

The Fretboard: Going Up and Down The Fretboard

2

On a single string, when you play lower-numbered frets, they are lower in pitch than when you play the higher-numbered frets. For example, when you play the 3rd fret (of any string), it is lower in pitch than the 5th fret (of the same string). The lowest pitch on any string is the open string itself. You can think of it as “fret 0,” the floor that “going up” starts from.

When people say “go UP the fretboard” or “move UP the fretboard”, they mean that you should go UP in pitch and therefore move toward the body of the instrument. Going “down the fretboard” means you play pitches that move toward the headstock.

What about going from string to string?

When going or moving from strings that are closer to your nose towards strings closer to your toes, you are also moving higher in pitch.

Generally, when people say “move to the next string UP” they are referring to pitch and therefore you need to move to the next string down physically on the fretboard. This is also consistent with how tablature works. In tablature, lower-pitched strings are at the bottom and the higher-pitched strings are at the top.

Guitar example

In this simple example below, you play the open 6th string 4 times (in measure 1) then you play the open 5th string 4 times (in measure 2). You, therefore, move UP a string from measure 1 to measure 2.

This example is in tablature. In Standard Notation, sometimes the string number is in a circle to explicitly tell you to go from one string (6th string, low E) to another string (5th string, A).

You don’t need to read this notation yet.

The two pictures above are just two different ways of writing music down: tablature (normally six lines for guitar; this cropped example shows the relevant lower strings) and standard notation (the five-line staff). For now, don’t worry about reading either one—the only thing to notice is that the marks move from the lower string in measure 1 to the next string up in measure 2. That’s the whole point of the example.

We’ll cover what the notes are actually called in the next chapter, and a later bonus section (Standard Notation) is devoted to reading the staff string-by-string. So if these pictures look like a foreign language right now, that’s completely expected.

Bass example

Bass tablature normally has four lines. The low E string is the bottom line, and the higher-pitched strings appear above it.

Play the open E string—the 4th string—four times in measure 1. Then play the open A string—the 3rd string—four times in measure 2. You have moved up one string.

You don’t need to read this tablature yet.

Each of its four lines represents one bass string. For now, notice only that the marks move from the bottom E-string line to the A-string line immediately above it.

 

Some teachers use “up” and “down” to describe the physical direction their hand moves rather than the change in pitch. That reverses the convention used in tablature. In this book, moving from a lower-pitched string to a higher-pitched string means moving up a string; moving toward a lower-pitched string means moving down.

Music Theory: Notes

3

On a fretless instrument, your finger can land anywhere along the string, allowing you to slide through the pitches between two locations. A fretted guitar or bass divides the string into fixed positions. Each fret gives you one specific pitch—one note. Fretless bassists can use those same locations as reference positions.

A note is a named pitch.

Notes are the basic building blocks of music. They’re kind of like colors in a painting. You can use a single color (note) to create part of a painting. Or you can combine colors (ie. notes) to make new colors (chords – multiple notes played at the same time).

One difference between art and music

You don’t really need to know the names of colors to create a beautiful painting. But knowing the names of notes and where they are on your instrument gives you a reliable way to find, describe, and reuse musical ideas.

Guitar application

On guitar, fretboard knowledge and shape knowledge work together when you play, compose, and improvise melodies and songs. Chapter 12 introduces the strategy for memorizing notes on the fretboard.

The 6th string—the low E closest to your nose—produces about 82.4 Hz. The 1st string—the high E closest to your toes—produces about 329.6 Hz. Chapter 1 pictured those frequencies.

Some tuners and tuner apps display a note’s frequency. Try the online tuner at https://tuner.ninja. Your open 1st string should display E at or near 329.6 Hz.

Bass application

On bass, note locations and fretboard shapes work together. In Level 1, memorizing the E, A, and D strings will help you find chord roots quickly. Root–5th–octave patterns then help you turn those roots into bass lines, fills, and melodies. Chapter 12 introduces the memorization method.

The open E string of a four-string bass produces a fundamental frequency of about 41.2 Hz. The open A string produces 55 Hz.

Some tuners and tuner apps display both the note name and its frequency. Try the online tuner at https://tuner.ninja with your open A string: it should display A at or near 55 Hz.

The Fretboard: Names of the Strings (the notes they produce)

4

We could name each pitch by the frequency they produce. For example, “Will you play the 440 Hz note for me so that I can tune my Violin?”

Instead, we’ve given each pitch a name. An open string—a string played without pressing a fret—produces a note, and that note is also the string’s name.

Guitar standard tuning

From nose to toes, the guitar strings are E, A, D, G, B, and E again. This is called “standard tuning” or “E Standard”.

The thinnest string, the high E closest to your toes, is String 1. The thickest string, the low E closest to your nose, is String 6. The complete mapping is E (6), A (5), D (4), G (3), B (2), E (1).

These are the pitches guitarists have agreed to use as a common starting point. Other tunings exist, including Drop D and DADGAD, but this book uses guitar standard tuning.

Bass standard tuning

From nose to toes, the strings of a standard four-string bass are E, A, D, and G. This is also called “standard tuning” or “E Standard”.

The thinnest string, the G closest to your toes, is String 1. The thickest string, the low E closest to your nose, is String 4. The complete mapping is E (4), A (3), D (2), G (1).

These are the pitches bassists have agreed to use as a common starting point. Other tunings exist, including Drop D, but this book uses four-string bass standard tuning.

Learn all four open-string names now. In Level 1, however, you will memorize fretted note locations only on E, A, and D. The G string may appear as the upper note of an interval shape, but its complete note map begins in Level 2.

Music Theory: Why Note Names Repeat

5

Two different pitches can have the same note name for much the same reason that “dark red” and “light red” are both considered red.

Guitar example

The guitar’s low E string produces about 82.4 Hz, while its high E string produces about 329.6 Hz. Their frequencies are separated by a factor of four: start at 82.4 Hz, double it to 164.8 Hz, then double it again to reach 329.6 Hz.

Those two doublings are why the low E sounds like a darker shade and the high E sounds like a lighter shade of the same note. They are different pitches, but both are named E.

Bass example

On a standard four-string bass, the open E string produces about 41.2 Hz. The E at the 12th fret produces about 82.4 Hz—exactly twice the frequency.

That doubling is why the open E sounds like a darker shade and the 12th-fret E sounds like a lighter shade of the same note. They are different pitches, but both are named E.

Remember how doubled frequencies sounded as though they “just fit” back in Chapter 1? Your ears hear every doubling as another version of the same note. This distance is called an octave, and the note name repeats at every octave.

Guitars and basses usually have fretboard dots, also called dot inlays, that help you see where a note name returns in a lighter shade.

The Fretboard: 12 Notes on a Single String and The Fret Markers

6

The fret markers (ie. dot inlays) or side dots help indicate when notes repeat. For example, the 12th fret of the low E string sounds like it “just fits” when played along with the open low E string (you play the 12th fret on the low E string and a friend plays the low E string unfretted). Therefore, the notes at the 12th fret get the same name as the open string. The 12th fret is usually marked with two dots. It’s like the fretboard starts again at the 12th fret but now everything is a “lighter shade” of the same notes.

This is also where the title of this chapter comes from. How many different notes are there on a single string before the names start repeating? Twelve. Here’s the part that trips people up: count the open string as your first note, the 1st fret as your second note, the 2nd fret as your third, and so on. By the time you reach the 12th fret you’ve played 13 notes but only 12 of them have different names. That 13th note, at the 12th fret, is where the open string’s name comes back as a “lighter shade”.

That’s “12 notes on a single string”, and it’s why the double dots land on the 12th fret.

This also helps explain the other fret markers. On many guitars and basses, the 3rd fret has a dot. Compare that note with the note exactly 12 frets higher, at the 15th fret. Do you notice how the 3rd-fret note sounds like a darker shade of the 15th-fret note?

Guitar examples

  1. The open E String and the 12th fret of the same string have the same name because they are 12 frets apart.
  2. The 3rd fret of the A string and the 15th fret of the A string have the same note name because they are 12 frets apart. Even though you don’t know the name of this note just yet, listen closely to how the note on the 15th fret sounds like a “lighter shade” of the note at the 3rd fret.
  3. The 2nd fret of the D string and the 14th fret of the D string have the same note name because they are 12 frets apart. The note at the 14th fret is a “lighter shade” of the note at the 2nd fret.
  4. The 1st fret of the B string and the 13th fret of the B string have the same note name because they are 12 frets apart. The note at the 13th fret is a “lighter shade” of the note at the 1st fret.

Bass examples—E, A, and D

  1. The open E string and the 12th fret of the E string have the same note name.
  2. The 3rd fret of the A string and the 15th fret of the A string have the same note name.
  3. The 2nd fret of the D string and the 14th fret of the D string have the same note name.

The G string is shown because it is part of the bass, but Level 1 does not ask you to memorize its fret-by-fret note locations.

So where do all these dots live? On most guitars and basses, you’ll find single dots at the 3rd, 5th, 7th, and 9th frets, and double dots at the 12th fret. (The same pattern repeats higher up and that’s why there’s a dot at the 15th fret in the examples above.) Their job is simple: they let you find your place on the neck at a glance, so you don’t have to count up fret-by-fret from the bottom every time.

Rhythm: The Beat, Measures, and Note Durations

7

Alongside pitch and notes, there is a third basic idea every musician needs: rhythm—when the notes happen and how long they last. You already feel rhythm in your body whenever you tap your foot to a song. These next two chapters give names to what your foot is doing. (We will use rhythm constantly once we start playing fretboard patterns and scales later in the book.)

The beat = the pulse of the song

The beat is the steady, even pulse you naturally tap your foot to. Watch your foot: it goes down, then up, then down, then up—a simple, repeating motion. That down-and-up of the foot is going to help us count every rhythm in these chapters, so get comfortable with it now.

The quarter note = one beat

In music we have a way to write down just one beat, and it is called the quarter note. A quarter note lasts exactly one beat. Every quarter note has two parts: the note head (the circle) and the stem (the line coming off it).

In standard notation a quarter note is a filled-in note head with a stem. In guitar or bass tablature, instead of a note head we write the fret number, and a stem comes down from it to show the rhythm. (If you use a program like Songsterr, a quarter note shows up as a fret number with a short line extending down from it.)

If you write eight quarter notes in a row, you count them out loud as you tap: “1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8.” Try it—put on a simple drum beat, and tap your foot once on every quarter note.

Then choose the open E, A, or D string and play one note on each tap. Use whichever plucking or picking technique is comfortable; concentrate on making every note last exactly one beat.

Measures and bar lines

Counting “1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8…” gets unwieldy fast, so we organize music into small chunks. We draw a vertical line through the staff called a bar line. The space between two bar lines is called a measure (also called a bar).

Here is the important part: when you reach a bar line, you do not keep counting upward. You start over at “1.” So eight quarter notes split into two measures are counted “1, 2, 3, 4 … 1, 2, 3, 4” instead of “1…8.” A double bar line at the very end tells you the music has finished. In tab, measures are usually numbered (1, 2, 3, 4…) so you can keep track of where you are.

The time signature

At the very start of a piece you will see two numbers stacked on top of each other. This is the time signature, and it tells you how the song’s rhythm is organized.

The top number tells you how many beats are in each measure. A 4 on top means four beats per measure; a 3 on top means three beats per measure; a 5 would mean five.

The bottom number tells you what kind of note gets one beat. Here is a trick: read the time signature as if it were a fraction. The signature 4/4 reads as “four-fourths.” Another word for a “fourth” is a quarter—so the bottom 4 is telling you the quarter note gets one beat. Put it together and 4/4 means: four beats in each measure, and the quarter note gets the beat. This is the most common time signature in popular music.

The half note = two beats

Now let’s build longer notes. If you combine two quarter notes into a single note, how many beats is it? Two (1 + 1 = 2). That new note is the half note. Its note head is empty (not filled in) and it has a stem. You count it across its full length: “1–2” or “3–4.” In tab, a half note often appears as a fret number inside a small circle with a half-length stem.

The whole note = four beats

Combine two half notes and you get the whole note: four beats long. It is drawn as just a circle with no stem. In 4/4 time a whole note “takes up the whole measure”—you play it once and let it ring for all four beats while your foot keeps tapping “1, 2, 3, 4.”

That is the core toolkit: the beat, the quarter note (1 beat), the half note (2 beats), and the whole note (4 beats), all organized into measures by a time signature. In the next chapter we add silence, faster notes, and a couple of ways to stretch a note’s value.

Rhythm: Rests, Eighth Notes, Ties, and Dots

8

Music is not only sound—it is also silence placed exactly in time. In this chapter we add rests (silence), eighth notes (faster notes), and two tools—ties and dots—for lengthening a note.

Rests = the sound of silence

A quarter note is one beat of sound. The opposite of sound is silence, and a rest is the symbol that tells you not to play—while the beat keeps right on going. Each note value has a matching rest:

  • The quarter rest = one beat of silence.
  • The half rest = two beats of silence. It looks like a small rectangle.
  • The whole rest = four beats of silence. It is the same rectangle shape as the half rest.

Because the half rest and whole rest look alike, use this visual mnemonic: the half rest sits on top of a line like a hat, while the whole rest hangs below a line. If you clap four quarter notes but the third beat is a quarter rest, you simply make no sound on beat 3—“1, 2, (rest), 4”—while your foot never stops tapping.

Guitar: make the rest silent

Choose an open E, A, or D string. Play on beats 1, 2, and 4, and stop the string from ringing on beat 3. The rest begins when the sound stops—not merely when you skip a new attack.

Bass: actively mute the rest

Choose an open E, A, or D string. Play on beats 1, 2, and 4, and actively mute the string with either hand on beat 3. A low bass note can keep ringing through the rest, so skipping the next pluck is not enough.

The eighth note = half a beat

To make a faster note, we split the quarter note in half. Since a quarter note is one beat, half of it is half a beat—so two eighth notes fit inside a single beat. Eighth notes have a flag, and when you write several in a row they are joined by a beam.

This is where the down-and-up of your foot pays off. Count eighth notes as “1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and.” The number happens when your foot is down; the “and” happens when your foot is up. So a full measure of eighth notes is: down-up, down-up, down-up, down-up, counted “1-and-2-and-3-and-4-and.”

There is also an eighth rest—half a beat of silence—which looks like a small flag on a short stem (a little like the letter “y”). If the last half-beat of a measure is an eighth rest, you still count the “and,” you just don’t play it. We often write that silent count in parentheses, like “…4 (and).”

Ties = connecting two notes

A tie is a curved line that connects two notes of the same pitch. You play the first note and hold it through the value of both—you do not play the second note again. For example, tying two quarter notes together gives you exactly the same sound as one half note. When counting, a tie is often shown with a hyphen, like “1–2.” Sometimes a songwriter writes a half note and sometimes two tied quarter notes—they can mean the same length; it just depends on the song.

Dots = add half the value

A dot placed after a note adds half of that note’s value. Think of it with plain numbers: dot a 4 and you add half of 4 (which is 2) to get 6; dot a 10 and you add 5 to get 15; dot an 80 and you add 40 to get 120.

Now apply that to notes. A half note is 2 beats; a dotted half note adds half of 2 (one beat) for a total of 3 beats—very handy in 3/4 time, where it fills a whole measure.

A dotted quarter note is a quarter note (1 beat) plus half its value (an eighth note, half a beat), for one-and-a-half beats. It creates the rhythm counted “1, 2-and, 3, 4,” where the held note absorbs the next half-beat. In fact, a dotted quarter note sounds identical to a quarter note tied to an eighth note—the dot is just a tidier way to write the same idea.

That is Level 1 rhythm. With the beat, measures, time signatures, the note values (whole, half, quarter, eighth) and their rests, plus ties and dots, you can read and count the rhythms behind the vast majority of popular songs—and, just as importantly, write down your own.

So, we have 12 notes in total. But how can we use this to make music?

Over the course of history, we have discovered a way to create a “palette” of notes to choose from in order to create songs. When you “paint” with this palette to create a melody or a song, it will sound great! In total, we will pick just 7 notes (colors).

You already know what this palette sounds like, even if you’ve never thought about it as “music theory”. It’s “Do Re Mi Fa So La Ti Do”. The pattern they teach you in kindergarten. The 7 notes (colors) we pick are exactly those: Do, Re, Mi, Fa, So, La, Ti, and then Do again (the “lighter shade” of the first one). So as you number the palette 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, you can also sing Do, Re, Mi, Fa, So, La, Ti.

The first step for creating this palette is choosing the “tonic” note or “key-note”. This is the note the whole palette is built around. It's the “home base” that a melody feels like it wants to settle back onto (it’s also the “Do” in “Do Re Mi”). Any note will do. For example, you could simply choose E as played by the low E string (or high E string). After you choose this note, you must follow this “interval sequence” to create your musical palette.

An interval is the measurement of distance between any two notes. These numbers represent the number of frets that you must move to find the next note for your palette. The 221-2221 interval sequence easy to remember because it looks like a phone number!

We’ll first think theoretically (away from the fretboard). After picking your first note, move up 2 of the 12 notes and use that note as the 2nd note in the palette. Next, move up another 2 (of the 12), then use that note as the 3rd note of the palette. And so on. I will also number each note as I go.

The rainbow-colored notes are what make up the “Major Scale”. The 221-2221 interval sequence can also be considered the “Major Scale Formula”. The black dots (in between the rainbow-colored dots) are still notes but they are not part of our “palette”. Again, the actual musical term for this “palette” is The Major Scale.

Notice that we could have started on any of the 12 notes. That means there are 12 different Major Scales (one starting on each note) but every single one of them is built with the same 221-2221 formula.

Music Theory: The C Major Scale

11

Let’s name some notes. The first Major scale to learn is the C Major scale. We use letters of the alphabet to name notes. And since there are only 7 notes in a Major scale, we are only going to use 7 letters. However, the first letter in the musical alphabet is C (I'll explain why later). Scale Degrees vs. Intervals

You may have noticed something. Back when we learned scale degrees, C was 1, D was 2, E was 3, and so on. Now that we’re counting intervals starting from C, we get the same numbers: C–D is a 2nd, C–E is a 3rd, C–F is a 4th. So a lot of beginning guitarists (I see this all over the guitar forums on Reddit) assume that scale degrees and intervals are the same thing. They are not.

Here’s the difference. A scale degree is a note’s fixed address inside a key. In the key of C, E is always the 3rd degree, period. It doesn’t matter what other note you happen to play it with. An interval, on the other hand, is relative. It’s the distance between any two notes, and you can measure it starting from any note you want, not just the tonic.

The numbers only line up when you happen to count from the tonic. Step off the tonic and they come apart. Take D and F. As scale degrees in the key of C, those are 2 and 4. But as an interval, D–F is a 3rd (D, E, F). The interval doesn’t care that D is the 2nd degree. It just measures the distance from D up to F. In fact, a 3rd can be built off of any note: C–E, D–F, E–G, and so on. They are all 3rds.

So here is the takeaway: scale degrees are fixed (they are always counted from the tonic), while intervals are movable (they can be measured between any two notes). They happen to match only when you start counting from the tonic, and that coincidence is exactly what trips people up.

Music Theory and The Fretboard: The Harmonic (Overtone) Series

14

What is so fascinating about intervals is that they occur in nature!

Introduction to The harmonic (overtone) Series

Scientists and music theorists like Pythagoras (6th century BCE) used tools like the “monochord” to discover that certain intervals occur in nature. The monochord is like a single-string guitar with a moveable bridge that allows you to significantly reduce the length of the string.  When you pluck a full length string and then pluck a second string that is exactly half in length of the first, the interval created between the two strings is an octave. They discovered that if you continue shortening the string in length (a third, a fourth, etc. of the original), each string creates a tone that is consonant to the previously played strings.

The very first tone produced by a full length string has a name: “the fundamental” or “first partial”. The second tone produced by a string half in length is called the “second partial” or “first overtone”. The third tone is called the “third partial” or “second overtone”. Relative to the second partial (or first overtone), it is up a 5th interval. The fourth partial produces a tone that is up a perfect 4th from the previous partial (the second overtone).

playing The overtone Series on the guitar

Here is how you play the first 3 overtones of the Harmonic Series.

  1. The open E string is the fundamental (or 1st partial).
  2. The harmonic at the 12th fret is the 1st overtone (or 2nd partial). It is up an octave (an 8th) interval and therefore it is the note E.
  3. The harmonic at the 7th fret is the 2nd overtone (or 3rd partial). It is up a fifth interval relative to the previous note and therefore it is the note B.
  4. And the harmonic at the 5th fret is the 3rd overtone (or 4th partial). It is up a fourth interval relative to the previous note and therefore it is the note E.

To play natural harmonics, lightly touch the low E string without fretting the string and pluck relatively forcefully for a full sound. You’ll play harmonics on the 12th, 7th, and 5th frets.

You may also have inadvertently discovered the overtone series when tuning your guitar. Other strings will sympathetically vibrate when you pluck the low E string. Sometimes your tuner will jump around and display notes other than “E”. These other mysterious notes are simply the tones from the harmonic series (overtone series). You can help your tuner out by muting the other strings with your free hand.

Music Theory: The Perfect Intervals

15

Each interval has 2 attributes:

  1. Quantity. This is the number of note names the interval spans. This is what we covered in detail in Chapter 13, “Intro To Intervals.”
  2. Quality. Quality describes the number of half steps traversed from the lower note to the higher note in the interval. The term that represents this number will come before the interval name. For example, “Perfect 4th”. “Perfect” represents the quality of the 4th interval. There are five different interval qualities: Major, Minor, Augmented, Diminished, and Perfect. In this book, we are only focusing on the perfect interval quality because it will help us understand Power Chords (chapter 16).

 

There are 4 intervals in the “Perfect” family: Unisons, 4ths, 5ths, and 8ths (also called the octave).

Why are these four called “perfect”? Think back to the overtone series from the last chapter. The very first tones nature stacks above a fundamental are the octave, then the 5th, then the 4th. Because these intervals come straight out of the harmonic series, they sound especially pure and stable to our ears, and that stability is what earned them the name “perfect”. (The unison counts too. It’s literally the same note, which is about as stable as you can get.)

The Perfect Unison Interval

In theory, the Unison interval might be a little more complicated than it is in the real world. This interval is the same note. For example, when you and a friend both play the E string, you are playing “in unison”. The distance from E to E is 0 (no half steps traversed) and only 1 note name is counted (just E). Therefore the interval is a called a Perfect Unison. The shorthand for this interval is P1.

The Perfect 4th Interval

A 4th interval is perfect if you traverse 5 half steps. For example, the interval from G to C is a 4th because it spans 4 note names. G (1), A (2), B (3), and C (4). Its quality is Perfect because you traverse 5 half steps. Therefore G–C is a Perfect 4th. The shorthand for this interval is P4.

The Perfect 5th Interval

A 5th interval is perfect if you traverse 7 half steps. For example, C to G is a Perfect 5th. The shorthand for this interval is P5.

The Perfect 8th Interval

And finally an octave interval (an 8th) is perfect if you traverse 12 half steps. For example, from the low C to the high C is an octave (or Perfect 8th). The shorthand for this interval is P8. As you know, this interval is also called the octave.

A chord is usually made up of 3 or more notes. But in the guitar/rock world, there is a chord called the Power Chord which is only 2 notes.

You’ll often hear that “chords come from scales”. And that’s a useful shortcut: the Major scale is a convenient place to find the notes that make up a chord (ie. the chord tones). But strictly speaking, a chord is built from intervals, not from a scale. The power chord comes from a single interval: the perfect 5th. And because a chord is built from a root with intervals stacked on it, you can start on any note, not just the first note of a scale. In other words, every note in the scale can be a root note for a chord. The root note is the note a chord is built on, and it’s the note the chord takes its name from. (A power chord built on C is a C power chord.)

These power-chord shapes are movable. The shape is just an interval relationship (a root and its perfect 5th), so you can slide it anywhere along these bass strings. Chord Symbols

Chord symbols give you two pieces of information: the root note and the intervals needed to create the rest of the chord. You will typically see chord symbols (and chord diagrams) above the tablature (or standard notation).  The first chord symbol we’ll learn about is the one for a Power Chord.

Here’s one important thing about actually playing these shapes: you only strum the strings that the shape sits on. A power chord on the bass strings is just 2 strings (the root and its 5th), so you don’t want to strum all six. If the extra strings ring out, the chord will sound muddy and confused. Music Theory: Why B–F is not a Perfect 5th

19

The interval B–F spans 5 note names. B (1), C (2), D (3), E (4), and F (5). Therefore, it is indeed a 5th interval.

But it’s a “Diminished 5th”, not a “Perfect 5th”. “Diminished” simply means one half step smaller than Perfect. As you know, a 5th is only Perfect when you traverse 7 half steps, and from B to F you only traverse 6. Use a single line of notes to count them, and remember to start counting from zero. B (0), C (1), unnamed black note (2), D (3), unnamed black note (4), E (5), and finally F (6). That’s only 6 half steps, one short of a Perfect 5th.

You can see the same thing on the fretboard. Compare the distance from E to B (7 frets) to the distance from B to F (just 6 frets). As discussed in previous chapters, E–B is a Perfect 5th, so B–F coming up one fret short is exactly what makes it Diminished.

The B–F interval, in the key of C, is the only 5th in the major scale that isn’t Perfect. Every other one (C–G, D–A, E–B, F–C, G–D, and A–E) is a Perfect 5th. And this isn’t special to the key of C. The 5th built on the 7th scale degree is always a Diminished 5th, in every Major key. It’s also the reason there are only 6 power chords in a key and not 7. You can’t build a power chord on the 7th note, because a power chord needs a Perfect 5th, and the 5th you get there is Diminished.

In summary, a 5th is only Perfect when you traverse 7 half steps. B–F traverses just 6, so it’s a Diminished 5th. And it will always be the 5th built on the 7th degree of the scale.

Music Theory: Inverting the Perfect Intervals

23

The Perfect Interval Family is:

  1. The Perfect Unison. It spans 1 note name and you traverse 0 half steps from the root tone to the next note in the interval.
  2. The Perfect 4th. It spans 4 note names and you traverse 5 half steps from the root tone to the next note in the interval.
  3. The Perfect 5th. It spans 5 note names and you traverse 7 half steps from the root tone to the next note in the interval.
  4. The Perfect 8th (or Octave). It spans 8 note names and you traverse 12 half steps from the root tone to the next note in the interval.

In this chapter, we’ll discuss what it means to “invert” an interval. There are only two notes in an interval (the lower note and the higher 2nd note). To invert an interval, simply change the position of the notes so the lower note now becomes the higher note.

G5 is G–D. The lower note is G. The higher note is D. To invert it, take the G and make it the high note. So now it is D–G. The low note is D. The high note is G.

Take a moment and analyze the interval D–G. How many note names does it span?

Just count the letter names spanned... It spans 4 notes.

 

How many half steps must you traverse to get from D to G? When counting half steps, you must count every note (including the out-of-key black notes).

So what kind of interval is it? It is a Perfect 4th!

  1. When you invert a Perfect 5th, it becomes a Perfect 4th interval.
  2. When you invert a Perfect 4th, it then becomes a Perfect 5th interval.

 

Inverting a Diminished 5th (like B–F) creates the interval F–B. The half steps traversed from F to B is 6 but a Perfect 4th is only 5 half steps. Therefore the 4th, F–B, is a little larger and therefore we call it an “Augmented 4th”.

Two simple rules cover what happens to an interval’s quality when you invert it. A Perfect interval stays Perfect. A Diminished and an Augmented interval swap, so the Diminished 5th becomes an Augmented 4th, and the Augmented 4th becomes a Diminished 5th.

There’s also a handy shortcut for the numbers. An interval and its inversion always add up to 9. A 4th inverts to a 5th (4 + 5 = 9), and a Unison (a 1st) inverts to an Octave (an 8th), since 1 + 8 = 9. (It comes to 9, not 8, because the note you flip gets counted in both intervals.)

And here’s why inversion works the way it does. An interval and its inversion always add up to one octave (12 half steps). A Perfect 5th (7 half steps) plus a Perfect 4th (5 half steps) equals 12. A Diminished 5th (6) plus an Augmented 4th (6) also equals 12.

Music Theory: Intro to Sharps, Naturals, Key Signatures, and the G Major Scale

So far, we’ve been learning and discussing the key of C, including the C Major scale, Power Chords, and chord progressions in that key. The notes in the key of C are called “natural” notes. In reality, the full and complete name of each note is “C natural”, “D natural”, etc. However, most people will leave the “natural” part of the name off and just simply say “C”, “D”, etc.

As you can see, the green F is not in the scale. Instead the unlabeled black note just before G should be in the scale as a labeled, “rainbow” note. What is the name of that note? The way that music theory solves this problem is by calling that unlabeled note “F♯” (pronounced “F sharp”). A sharp raises a note by one half step, so F♯ is the note one half step above F. On a fretted guitar or bass, that means moving one fret higher on the same string. We still need to have all 7 letters represented in the scale. We also need to indicate that the note is not F♮(“natural”).

Now both major scale attributes are accounted for:

  1. The Major Scale must have each note name present. None are skipped and none are used twice. Even though the note, F, has a sharp symbol next to it, all 7 letter names are used (G, A, B, C, D, E, and F).
  2. The scale must follow the “221-2221” (or wwh-w-wwh) interval pattern.

Music Theory: Key of D (two sharps)

28

So far, we’ve learned about the key of C and its Major Scale…

…the key of G and its Major Scale…

Now, let’s build the D Major Scale. But let’s first see if we can notice any patterns when we built the G Major Scale.

Perhaps you noticed that G is a 5th from C. Count it out: C-D-E-F-G…1, 2, 3, 4, 5.

Similarly, D is a 5th from G. Count it out: G-A-B-C-D…1, 2, 3, 4, 5.

If “going” from C to G meant that we need to add 1 sharp to our key signature, does that mean we’ll need to add another sharp when building the D Major Scale?

  • C has no sharps (or flats).
  • G has 1 sharp.
  • Therefore, D must have 2 sharps? Let’s check…

 

When you apply the 221-2221 formula, you’ll need a half step between two sets of scale degrees: the 3rd–4th and the 7th–1st. In other words, those notes must be right next to each other. I start on D (the tonic or key-note). Then I use arrows to show you that I applied the 221-2221 formula.

In the above graphic, the 3rd labeled note is F♮(natural) and the 4th labeled note is G♮(natural). But they are separated by an unnamed note (ie. a whole step apart). The 221-2221 formula tells us that we need the note that is in-between those two notes. We’ll call it F♯ (sharp). And we’ll put F♮(natural) away and let it become a black note that is not part of the scale.

Next, you’ll also notice that C and D are a whole step apart (ie. separated by an unnamed note). Again, the 221-2221 formula tells us that we need to use the note in-between C♮(natural) and D♮(natural). Let’s call it C♯. We’ll then put C♮(natural) away and let it become a black note that is not part of the scale.

So, yes the pattern fits! The key of D has two sharps.

Notice what happened: the key of D kept the F♯ from the key of G and added just one new sharp, C♯. That’s the pattern. Each time you go up a 5th to a new key, you keep the sharps you already had and add exactly one more.

And that new sharp is always the 7th note of the new scale, a half step below the tonic. F♯ is the 7th note of G Major, and C♯ is the 7th note of D Major.

Notice a pattern?

Before moving on, take note of how many sharped notes there are in each of the keys that we have studied so far. C has 0 sharps (of course). G has 1. D has 2. A has 3. E has 4. Notice a pattern?

What is the interval from C to G? C (1), D (2), E (3), F (4), G (5). C to G is a fifth. Similarly, G to D is a fifth. D to A is a fifth. And so on.

If E to B is a fifth, then that must mean the B major scale has just one more sharped note than E for a total of 5 sharps. And the F♯ major scale then must have a total of 6 sharps.

There’s also a pattern to which notes get sharped. The sharps always appear in this order: F♯, C♯, G♯, D♯, A♯, E♯. So A Major (3 sharps) uses the first three (F♯, C♯, G♯), and E Major (4 sharps) adds the next one (D♯). That order is itself a chain of 5ths: F-C-G-D-A-E.

One more thing, the note that is different between the C Major scale and the G Major scale is the 7th scale degree of the G Major scale. So a shortcut to create the G Major scale would be:

  1. Write out the C Major scale but start from G: G – A – B – C – D – E – F – G
  2. Make the 7th scale degree sharp: G – A – B – C – D – E – F♯ – G

Neat, huh?

So we now have the G Major scale written out. To create the D Major scale, write out the G Major scale but start with D.

  1. D – E – F♯ – G – A – B – C – D.
  2. Then make the 7th scale degree sharp. It is now D – E – F♯ – G – A – B – C♯ – D which is the correct D Major scale.

Some Important Terms

31

To get started with learning how to read standard notation, there are a few terms we need to define.

Beat = the pulse of music

The beat of music is like your heart’s pulse. It is steady and consistent.  If you listen to any song, you should be able to tap your foot to the beat of the song.

 

Quarter note = 1 beat of music

Written notes in sheet music not only tell you the pitch (ie. which fret of the guitar) but also how long you should play that note. The time it takes from one beat of music to occur up until the very next beat is exactly how long a quarter note should be played. The easiest quarter note to play is the one that is synced with the beat of the music.

 

Music Staff

The music staff has 5 lines and 4 spaces. Higher pitches are at the top of the staff. Lower pitches are at the bottom of the staff.

 

Bar Lines (or measure lines)

Bar lines (the vertical lines seen below) divide the music staff into measures. When music is divided into measures, it makes it easy to communicate with your bandmates where you want to start or end because they are often numbered.

 

Measures

Each measure contains a certain amount of beats. A common amount is simply 4 beats in a single measure. However, we will also learn to read music that has 2 beats in a measure and 3 beats in a measure. The quarter note gets its name because it’s very common for music to have 4 beats in a measure and therefore each beat is a quarter of the entire measure 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 = 1 (or the whole measure). So in this case, if a note = beat, then it is a quarter note.

 

Time Signature

There are two aspects to a time signature. First, the “time signature” tells us how many beats are in each measure. Again, it’s common to have just 4 but it’s possible to have 2, 3, 5, 6, or 7 beats in a measure too.

Second, the time signature tells us which type of note gets the pulse of the music. Again, the most common is the quarter note = 1 beat of music.

The top number = number of beats per measure (4). Bottom number = the type of note that gets the beat (1/4 note).

 

Double Bar

A double bar indicates the end of a piece of music or a transition within the music. The double bar is on the far right.

 

Counting

Here is a piece of music that lacks pitch and only indicates rhythm. With this type of sheet music, you can play a single note, a single drum, or other percussion instruments. Its time signature is “4/4” which means there are 4 beats per measure and that the quarter note gets the beat. Above each note, I’ve indicated how you should “count” the music. Notice that you start over with each measure. Feel free to clap this rhythm and count as you clap. Are you able to do so with a steady and consistent pulse?

Now, try playing the above with the just open E string. Tap your foot and play the open E string at the same time. Are you able to play with a steady pulse? Are you able to play the E string on the guitar and tap your foot at the same time? These are skills that I learned to do in elementary school on my first instrument (the saxophone). And so I believe that all guitarists should learn to tap their foot while the play. It will help you develop an internal sense of rhythm.

 

The Treble Clef

There are a variety of “clefs” and their job is to tell us which notes the spaces and lines represent. The Treble Clef symbol (at the very far left) tells us that the lines of the music staff represent the notes (from bottom to top): E, G, B, D, and F. It also tells us that the spaces of the music staff represent the notes (from bottom to top): F, A, C, and E.

I would bet that you are familiar with the song “Do-Re-Mi” from the musical “The Sound of Music”. Even if you haven’t watched the movie, chances are you know the catchy tune: “Do, a deer, a female deer / Re, a drop of golden sun…”

While the lyrics may seem lighthearted and reminiscent of a nursery rhyme, they actually serve a significant purpose in teaching the fundamental musical skill known as solfège.

“Solfège” is heavily used in choir classes and private singing lessons. It is the tool that music teachers use to help the student develop their “musical ear”. However, many music students who go through a typical American education never learn how to develop their musical ear. Instead, their teachers require them to be very good note readers and performers. Of course there’s nothing wrong with performing written music from a page. But the most important aspect of becoming a musician is learning how to audiate. Audiation is the ability to produce the music on your instrument that you can imagine or “hear” in your head.

Solfège is one of the primary tools that you will use to learn how to audiate. I will provide you with some practical exercises that you can start incorporating into your daily routine.

Solfège is a naming system that assigns a unique name to each note in a scale. It utilizes a method called solmization, which assigns a single syllable (such as Do or Re) to each scale degree. These syllables differ from the actual note names (such as C or D).

The most commonly used solfège scale in music education is the major scale, which consists of the syllables Do, Re, Mi, Fa, Sol, La, Ti, and Do again. Because these syllables match the scale degree, they can be applied to any major scale, regardless of the key. For example, both the C major scale and the E major scale would use the same solfège syllables in the exact same order. In the key of C major, Do represents the note C, whereas in the key of E major, Do represents the note E. In C major, Re represents the note D. In E major it represents the note F♯ (“F-sharp”).

“Do” changes depending on the key you are in, hence the term “movable Do”. This concept is beneficial as it allows you to internalize the relative nature of every scale without having to sing every scale.

How To Do The Exercises

Each exercise has a high part and a low part. The high part is for people who are sopranos or altos. The low part is for people who are tenors, baritones, or basses.

 

  1. Play the guitar part that is appropriate for your voice—either the top line or the bottom line. Play those few notes and memorize the melody.
  2. Then do your best to sing the notes without playing at all. At first, you will be off. That is okay. You must learn to sing without using a crutch. The more you exercise this “audiation” muscle, the better you will get at it.
  3. However, for the “Do – Mi – Sol” exercises, I included the C Major chord for you to play to accompany yourself because you will be singing the chord tones of that chord. It’s important to learn how to sing one of the notes in a chord and get a sense of how that feels.
  4. If you absolutely must, play the guitar and sing the notes at the same time (in unison). Try to avoid doing that but it’s understandable if you need to do that at first.
  5. If you cannot match the pitch on the guitar, see if you can find a friend to sing the note. Then try to match their voice. Sometimes it can be easier to match the pitch from someone else’s voice than from an instrument.

And because of this natural phenomenon, Level 1 dives deep into these intervals and how they form the essential components of Power Chords. Here is a link to that chapter.

Going back to the Harmonic (Overtone) Series, the next two intervals that occur are the Major 3rd and Minor 3rd.

You can hear the interval jump by playing higher up the harmonic series on the low E string of the guitar. Fret “3.2” is just beyond the 3rd fret.

The 3rd Interval is part of the “Imperfect Family” of intervals. 2nds, 3rds, 6ths, and 7ths are all imperfect intervals. Whereas Unisons, 4ths, 5ths, and 8ths are part of the “Perfect Interval Family”. We’ll start with discussing the 3rd interval because the G string and the B string are a 3rd interval apart. Therefore it’s important we explore some of the interval and chord shapes that happen on the treble strings (string set 3-2-1).

The imperfect intervals are typically thought of as either “Major” or “Minor”. So what is the difference between the “Major 3rd” and the “Minor 3rd”? As we know from Level 1, the number of letter names spanned in an interval is its quantity (the general classification). Therefore both the Major and Minor 3rd intervals must span 3 note names.

However the interval quality is not the same between the two. The interval quality of the Major 3rd, being the number of half steps traversed, is bigger than the Minor 3rd. You must traverse 4 half steps in a Major 3rd but only 3 half steps in a Minor 3rd.

The first Major 3rd interval we’ll look at is G–B. G–B spans 3 note names— G (1), A (2), and B (3). And you must traverse 4 half steps from G to B— G, G♯(1), A (2), A♯(3), B (4). You can verify this on the G string (3rd string) on the guitar. The 4th fret of the G string is the note B.

Typically, the 3rd fret of any guitar will have a dot inlay fret marker. This particular fret marker indicates the Minor 3rd interval relative to each open string. On the E strings, the 3rd fret is the note G. E–G is a Minor 3rd interval because it spans 3 note names and you only need to traverse 3 half steps—E, F (1), F♯(2), G (3).

Major is bigger than Minor. Or Major = Big and Minor = Little.

Think of the Big Dipper and Little Dipper asterisms (which are recognizable patterns within a constellation). The Big Dipper is part of Ursa Major constellation and the Little Dipper is part of Ursa Minor constellation.

Tonic vs. Root. Why do I use the term "Tonic"?

2

In school, I’ve been taught that Tonic refers to the first note of the scale. And “root” refers to the foundational note of a chord.

But many people use “root” to refer to both (first note of a scale and the foundational note of a chord). I think this confuses beginners. I recommend that teachers use the correct terminology and stop using root to refer to the first note of a scale. Here are 6 reasons why.

 

  1. Musicians have used tonic to uniquely identify the first note of a scale for hundreds of years. Certainly, beginners have had no issues with that term prior to the invention of the internet. The book Elson’s Pocket Music Dictionary was published in 1909 and correctly defines each term.
    • “Tonic. The key-note of any scale; the chief, fundamental ground-tone, or first note, of the scale.” (p. 149)
    • “Root. The fundamental note of any chord.” (p. 126)
  2. Beginners actually get confused when you use root to both uniquely identify the first note of the scale and as the fundamental note of a chord. When beginners see a chord chart with a simple progression like 1-4-5 in the key of C, they will sometimes ask “For the C Major chord, do I play the C Major scale? And then for the F Major chord, do I play the F Major scale? And then for the G Major chord, do I play the G Major scale?”
    • This is obviously wrong. But how can you blame them? They’re only being logical, in my opinion.
  3. There are lots of terms in music that are much more confusing. There’s no need to try to change just 1 word when we use thousands of other complex jargon-y terms. It’s a better strategy to find a way to teach beginners so that they understand music theory with the correct terminology.
  4. Not one person who consistently uses the word “root” has ever told me that I should stop using the word tonic. Because they know that I’m not wrong. I’m using the correct terminology that is used in schools, on the AP Music Theory test, and at colleges and universities.
  5. Every note is a root note! When you understand that a root note is the foundational or fundamental note for a chord, then it doesn’t make sense to uniquely identify the 1st note of a scale as “the root”.
  6. Every scale degree has a name! Tonic, Supertonic, Mediant, Subdominant, Dominant, Submediant, and Leading Tone. Nobody has ever suggested we stop using the other 6 names. Why are people misusing just one of them—the tonic—and not the rest?

I think people use root for both because they say that chords are built from scales. This isn’t entirely correct. Yes, you can use a scale to quickly identify chord tones. But the truth is that both chords and scales are built from intervals. A Major Triad is found in a variety of keys and modes (Major, Minor, etc) and is built with the formula: a Major 3rd + a Minor 3rd. In a Major key, you’ll find the Major Triad built from the 1st, 4th, and 5th scale degrees. A Major key is Major because the tonic chord is Major.

Maybe everyone should start using the term key-note instead? I think it’s great! It tells you that the key is named after this note. And everyone knows that the key-note speaker or presentation is the first one to happen! Or just use Tonic. Either way, I think using “root” to refer to both concepts hinders students’ understanding of music because eventually they will learn about “tonic” and will wonder why you never used this term.
