A Clear and Strategic Music Theory Learning Roadmap

Updated February 1, 2024.

Hello! My name is Denisse Vallecillos and I’m a teacher, conductor, and professional violinist. I have a Bachelor’s Degree in Music Education and a Master’s Degree in Orchestral Conducting. I teach and direct orchestra ensembles at all levels. However, for 5 years and with the help from a local guitar teacher (Chris Paul), I taught a High School-level introductory guitar class.

When I started the class, I did a lot of research on pedagogy and guitar methods. The recommended learning path as outlined by the National Association for Music Education is one that is based on classical music and performing as an ensemble. I was already directing 5 orchestras and just did not have the time to take on yet another performing ensemble. Additionally, the guitar class was unofficially the pre-requisite for the AP Music Theory class if students wanted to learn some foundational concepts first. Therefore I decided that the class would focus on popular music. After about 8 to 12 weeks (depending on the size of the class), we were strumming along to Hotel California. It was a very popular class because we had fun! And yes, we still learned how to read standard notation (sheet music). If you purchase the ebook, one of the bonus sections will teach you how (and why) you should learn this valuable skill.

Anyway, this series of books (Music Theory and The Fretboard) simply contain my theory lesson plans that I taught to my high school students. And it’s because I found so much success with my classes that I felt like other people would like the way that Chris and I explain these theory concepts.

Yes, “level 2” is coming out soon!

Who are you? Who this book is for…

This book is for the guitarist who has heard a lot about the importance of learning theory but does not know where to start. Ideally, you can play several open position chords (“cowboy chords”), play the rhythm part to some songs, and play a few riffs and scales. However, you don’t really know the fretboard that well and instead you rely on shapes.

But you want a better understanding of what exactly it is that you’re playing.

The result this book can help you achieve

My goal is to help the student create a strong foundation first. One of the things I noticed that most guitar students lack is a clear understanding of harmony. According to Aaron Copland’s “What to Listen for in Music” (first published in 1939), the four elements of music are rhythm, melody, harmony, and tone color (timbre). Much of music theory education is the study of harmony because of how complicated it can get. However, you don’t need to know everything about music theory in order to start applying it in real life.

The second thing I have discovered about the guitar world is that many educational resources teach you just the shortcuts, patterns, and shapes. There’s nothing wrong with shortcuts, patterns, and shapes. However, I view music theory as “the product”. While guitar-specific systems like CAGED are “the packaging”. An ideal learning pathway is one that has both—the fundamental music concepts and the shortcuts/patterns/shapes.

I have found a way to simplify the music theory learning pathway that guarantees a true understanding of how music works.

Ultimately, the result you will attain will be a confidence that comes from a solid foundation of competence in harmonic knowledge and fretboard mastery.

How and why my roadmap works

There’s no new information in this book that you can’t find elsewhere. However, I’ve carefully put together the information in a specific order to maximize efficiency and minimize overwhelm.

It’s very easy to write a boring theory book that covers an overwhelming set of concepts. It seems some authors are afraid of being criticized for leaving some stuff out.

The secret to finding rapid success is to focus on the most relevant information that will give you the most results possible. I have found that many well-meaning guitar teachers cannot resist the urge to include less-useful concepts (for beginners), like modes, in their introductory-level books. I purposefully exclude some concepts because the truth is that you can sound pretty good on the guitar when you get really good at the fundamentals. After you have mastered the fundamentals, you’ll be better equipped to learning more complex concepts.

This Level 1 book covers 2-note chords (power chords) and the bass strings (E, A, D). The Level 2 book will cover 3-note chords (triads) across all 6 strings. And the Level 3 book will cover 4-note chords (6ths and 7ths).

This book takes the abstract (theory) and the concrete (shapes) and puts them side-by-side. Look through the book; you’ll notice that each theory concept has a dedicated “theory” chapter and a dedicated “fretboard” chapter. I plan to keep the “theory” chapters light on words. If I can succinctly define each concept, then you can spend more time on the “fretboard” chapters studying the shapes.

The Three Pillars: How Music (and This Book) Fits Together

2

Before we start, here is the whole map on one page. Aaron Copland (whom I mentioned a moment ago) named four elements of music: rhythm, melody, harmony, and tone color (timbre). Tone color—why a guitar and a trumpet sound different on the same note—is wonderful, but it isn’t something you build a song out of. So this book focuses on the three elements you actually compose with:

Harmony. Melody. Rhythm.

Everything in this book serves one of those three. And each pillar breaks down into three simple parts:

Harmony — notes played together (the chords under a song)

  • Chord construction — what notes make a chord. We start with the simplest one possible: the power chord (just a root and a 5th).
  • Chords in a key — which chords belong together, so they sound good in the same song.
  • Progressions & voicings — putting chords in an order that moves, and choosing where on the neck to play them.

Melody — notes played in sequence (the part you sing)

  • Phrasing — where a musical line breathes; where it begins and ends.
  • Melodic shape — the contour of a line, its rise and fall.
  • Note choice — which notes you pick against the underlying harmony. This is where scales and intervals pay off.

Rhythm — where every note sits in time (the pillar the other two stand on)

  • Beat & tempo — the pulse, and how fast it goes.
  • Note duration — how long each note is held; long notes versus short ones.
  • Meter — how beats group together (time signatures, and counting along).

Where the “theory” fits

You may be wondering where things like note names, the fretboard, intervals, and reading notation live in this picture. They aren’t a fourth pillar—they are the shared toolkit that all three pillars draw on. Knowing the notes and the fretboard is what lets you build a chord (harmony), choose a note (melody), and place it in time (rhythm). That is why we cover them first: they are the tools, not the goal.

Why we start with power chords

Most theory books begin harmony with the triad (a three-note chord). We start one rung lower, with the power chord—two notes, one shape, and no “major or minor” quality to reason about yet. It is the simplest possible chord, and you can play real songs with it almost immediately. Once power chords are second nature, a triad is just a power chord with one note added. We climb the ladder one rung at a time, on purpose, so nothing ever feels like a leap.

Keep these three pillars in the back of your mind as you read. Every chapter is a tool for, or a piece of, Harmony, Melody, or Rhythm.

Music Theory: Pitch

1

“Pitch is the quality that makes it possible to judge sounds as ‘higher’ and ‘lower’ in the sense associated with musical melodies.” https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_(music)

You can feel this phenomenon in your throat when you sing along with songs. As you sing higher pitches, the larynx will rise in the vocal tract.

As you sing lower pitches, it will move downward.

Sound engineers have determined that each pitch produced by a musical instrument (including the human voice) has a “fundamental frequency”. Frequencies can be represented visually as “waves”. An excellent website that you can use to see frequencies visually represented is https://academo.org/demos/wave-interference-beat-frequency.

Although the waves are not moving on that website, try to imagine them going from left to right. “Frequency” is the number of waves that pass by in a second.

Here are two waves. The unit of measurement is called “Hertz” which is abbreviated as “Hz”. The top wave’s frequency is 329.6 Hz. The bottom wave’s frequency is 164.8 Hz (exactly half of 329.6 Hz). Because the 2nd frequency is exactly half of the first, its wavelength is exactly twice as long.

I recommend that you switch on the sound to hear what these frequencies sound like on that website. You’ll notice that a frequency that is exactly twice (or half) as much as the other will sound like it “just fits” with the other frequency.

Here is another example of two frequencies that “just fit”. The top frequency is still 329.6 Hz. The bottom one is a one-fourth of that—82.4 Hz.

These two frequencies sound like the “just fit” as well because the top frequency is a multiple of 2 (4x) of the bottom one.

The Fretboard: Going Up and Down The Fretboard

2

On a single string, when you play lower-numbered frets, they are lower in pitch than when you play the higher-numbered frets. For example, when you play the 3rd fret (of any string), it is lower in pitch than the 5th fret (of the same string).

When people say “go UP the fretboard” or “move UP the fretboard”, they mean that you should go UP in pitch and therefore move towards the sound hole. And going “down the fretboard” means you play notes that move towards the headstock.

What about going from string to string?

When going or moving from strings that are closer to your nose towards strings closer to your toes, you are also moving higher in pitch.

Generally, when people say “move to the next string UP” they are referring to pitch and therefore you need to move to the next string down physically on the fretboard. This is also consistent with how tablature works. In tablature, lower-pitched strings are at the bottom and the higher-pitched strings are at the top.

In this simple example below, you play the open 6th string 4 times (in measure 1) then you play the open 5th string 4 times (in measure 2). You, therefore, move UP a string from measure 1 to measure 2.

This example is in tablature. In Standard Notation, sometimes the string number is in a circle to explicitly tell you to go from one string (6th string, low E) to another string (5th string, A).

You don’t need to read this notation yet.

The two pictures above are just two different ways of writing music down: tablature (the four lines, where each line is a string) and standard notation (the five-line staff). For now, don’t worry about reading either one—the only thing to notice is that the marks move from the lower string in measure 1 to the next string up in measure 2. That’s the whole point of the example.

We’ll cover what the notes are actually called in the next chapter, and a later bonus section (Standard Notation) is devoted to reading the staff string-by-string. So if these pictures look like a foreign language right now, that’s completely expected.

 

However, some online YouTube teachers will refer to the physical orientation of the fretboard when they say “go down a string”. To them, they want you to consider the orientation of the guitar when speaking about moving across strings. The above example, for them, is moving down because you are ignoring pitch and only considering the orientation of the guitar. This is the opposite of how tablature works. When moving from a lower-pitched string to a higher-pitched string, you are moving up. This is also consistent with players who lay the guitar flat on their laps (lap steel guitarists). Despite the orientation of a lap steel guitar, they move up and down on the instrument even though the instrument is flat.

Music Theory: Notes

3

A note is a named pitch. Notes are the basic building blocks of music. They’re kind of like colors in a painting. You can use a single color (note) to create part of a painting. Or you can combine colors (ie. notes) to make new colors (chords – multiple notes played at the same time).

One difference between art and music

You don’t really need to know the names of colors to create a beautiful painting. But knowing the names of notes and where they are on the instrument helps you as a musician in a lot of ways. The reason why violinists can play wherever they want on their fingerboard is because they know where their notes are on the instrument. On the guitar, you can leverage both fretboard knowledge and shape knowledge to play, compose, and improvise your own melodies and songs. (Chapter 10 discusses the best strategy for memorizing notes on the fretboard.)

I’ve mentioned already that sound engineers have determined that each pitch produced by a musical instrument has a fundamental frequency. The 6th string (the one closest to your nose) of the guitar produces a frequency of 82.4 Hz. The 1st string (closest to your toes) produces a frequency of 329.6 Hz. I depicted these frequencies in Chapter 1.

Some tuners and tuner apps will tell you the frequency of the note you are playing. Check out the online tuner at https://tuner.ninja. Your 1st string should produce a pitch of 329.6 Hz.

The Fretboard: Names of the Strings (the notes they produce)

4

We could name each pitch by the frequency they produce. For example, “Will you play the 440 Hz note for me so that I can tune my Violin?”

Instead, we’ve given each pitch a name. Each string of the guitar produces a note and has a name. From nose to toes, the names of the strings are E, A, D, G, B, and E again. This is called “standard tuning” or “E Standard”.

Music Theory: Why are there two E strings on the guitar?

5

The reason why there are two E strings is a lot like why “dark red” and “light red” are both considered red.

The 6th string of the guitar produces a frequency of 82.4 Hz and the 1st string produces a frequency of 329.6 Hz. Their fundamental frequencies are separated by a factor of 4. This nice even relationship (a doubling twice over) is the reason why the low E is a “darker shade” and the high E string is a “lighter shade” of the same color. Even though they produce different pitches—the 6th string is a very low pitch and the 1st is a very high pitch—they get the same name.

We won’t be able to name all the notes on the guitar or piano if we gave every note a unique name. So therefore, names of notes will repeat when pitches have frequencies that are exactly twice as much.

The guitar has “dots” on the fretboard (also called “dot inlays”) that can help you see when a note’s name repeats again at its “lighter shade”.

 

The Fretboard: 12 Notes on a Single String and The Fret Markers

6

The fret markers (ie. dot inlays) or side dots help indicate when notes repeat. For example, the 12th fret of the low E string sounds like it “just fits” when played along with the open low E string (you play the 12th fret on the low E string and a friend plays the low E string unfretted). Therefore, the notes at the 12th fret get the same name as the open string. This is why there are two dots on the 12th fret. It’s like the fretboard starts again at the 12th fret but now everything is a “lighter shade” of the same notes.

This also helps explain the logic of the other dots. When you play the note at the 3rd fret (typically many guitars have a dot here), try playing the fret at exactly 12 frets higher (which is the 15th fret). Do you notice how the 3rd fret note sounds like a darker shade of the 15th fret note (which is the lighter shade)?

  1. The open E String and the 12th fret of the same string have the same name because they are 12 frets apart.
  2. The 3rd fret of the A string and the 15th fret of the A string have the same note name because they are 12 frets apart. Even though you don’t know the name of this note just yet, listen closely to how the note on the 15th fret sounds like a “lighter shade” of the note at the 3rd fret.
  3. The 2nd fret of the D string and the 14th fret of the D string have the same note name because they are 12 frets apart. The note at the 14th fret is a “lighter shade” of the note at the 2nd fret.
  4. The 1st fret of the B string and the 13th fret of the B string have the same note name because they are 12 frets apart. The note at the 13th fret is a “lighter shade” of the note at the 1st fret.

Music Theory: The Major Scale creates a palette of 7 colors (notes)

7

If notes repeat every 12 frets, then that means there are only 12 notes in total. You don’t have to know the names of the notes just yet to understand this concept because you can hear it. Play each fret on any string (but stay on that string). Start with the first fret. Here is an example on the high E string:

As you play each fret, count the fret 1, 2, 3…..10, 11, 12… and once you reach fret 13, the sound produced is the lighter shade of “1”. By the way, the open string is considered fret 0.

So, we have 12 notes in total. But how can we use this to make music?

Over the course of history, we have discovered a way to create a “palette” of notes to choose from in order to create songs. When you “paint” with this palette to create a melody or a song, it will sound great! In total, we will pick just 7 notes (colors).

The first step for creating this palette is choosing the “tonic” note or “key-note”. Any note will do. For example, you could simply choose E as played by the low E string (or high E string). After you choose this note, you must follow this “interval sequence” to create your musical palette.

An interval is the measurement of distance between any two notes. These numbers represent the number of frets that you must move to find the next note for your palette. The 221-2221 interval sequence easy to remember because it looks like a phone number!

We’ll first think theoretically (away from the fretboard). After picking your first note, move up 2 notes and use that note as the 2nd note in the palette. Next, move up another 2, then use that note as the 3rd note of the palette. And so on. I will also number each note as I go.

The rainbow-colored notes are what make up the “Major Scale”. The 221-2221 interval sequence can also be considered the “Major Scale Formula”. The black dots are still notes but they are not part of our “palette”. Again, the actual musical term for this “palette” is The Major Scale.

The Half Step Interval

When you move from one note to the next (or one fret to the next), you are performing what is called a “half step”. There is a half step between ANY note and the very next note.

But in the Major Scale specifically, there is only a half step between notes 3 to 4 and between notes 7 and the “lighter shade” 1. Half steps just naturally occur from 3–4 and from 7–1. In the 221-2221 sequence, the “1” represents a half step (“H”).

A whole step is 2 half steps.

Another common way to describe the Major Scale formula is to use a series of W’s and H’s (whole steps and half steps). 2 = a whole step. 1 = a half step. Therefore 221-2221 becomes WWH-WWWH. Now you can see there are two “WWH” chunks and they are connecting with a single “W”.

Music Theory: The C Major Scale

9

Let’s name some notes. The first Major scale to learn is the C Major scale. We use letters of the alphabet to name notes. And since there are only 7 notes in a Major scale, we are only going to use 7 letters. However, our first letter is C. So the 7 letters we use to name notes in music are C, D, E, F, G, A, and B. As you can see, when we get to G, we loop back to A.

When writing or playing scales, we like to play the tonic (the first note of the scale) again to provide a sense of completeness. Therefore, the C Major scale would be played as C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C.

Remember that the generic Major Scale pattern (in theory) looks like this. The numbered notes are called “scale degrees”. In college-level textbooks, they will have a caret above each number: 1̂, 2̂, 3̂, etc.

Again, there will always be a half step between the 3rd note and the 4th note of the scale. Notice how “3” and “4” are right next to each other? Additionally, there is a half step when going from the 7th note of the scale to the 1st note (at a “lighter shade”) again.

With that in mind, when we apply the names to the notes (starting with C)…

…half steps between E–F and B–C just naturally occur. Remember that important fact—half steps naturally occur between the E–F notes and the B–C notes.

Key of C

These palette of notes are what make up the “Key of C”. The note “C” is the tonic of the C Major scale and the key-note of the Key of C. (Elson’s Pocket Music Dictionary, p. 149)

The first step to becoming an intermediate-advanced player is memorizing the note locations on the fretboard.

It seems like a daunting task, I know. But there is a specific strategy that has a 100% success rate. Just do one note at a time.

On your guitar, play the 8th fret (of the low E string) and say/think “C”. Then play the 3rd fret (of the A string) and say/think “C”. Finally, play the 10th fret (of the D string) and say/think “C”. Then go back down to the 3rd fret (of the A string) and start over.

There are a few reasons why you should learn the bass strings (E, A, D). First, it’s only half of the fretboard and therefore is not as overwhelming as tackling the entire thing. Second, it’s very useful because many guitarists are quite familiar with Power Chord and Barre Chord shapes. If you memorize the notes on these strings, then you will know where all of your root notes are for all of those shapes.

Remember that note length and tempo work together: the same actual duration can be written as a longer note at a faster tempo or a shorter note at a slower one. Music Theory and The Fretboard: The Harmonic (Overtone) Series

12

What is so fascinating about intervals is that they occur in nature!

Introduction to The harmonic (overtone) Series

Scientists and music theorists like Pythagoras (6th century BCE) used tools like the “monochord” to discover that certain intervals occur in nature. The monochord is like a single-string guitar with a moveable bridge that allows you to significantly reduce the length of the string.  When you pluck a full length string and then pluck a second string that is exactly half in length of the first, the interval created between the two strings is an octave. They discovered that if you continue shortening the string in length (a third, a fourth, etc. of the original), each string creates a tone that is consonant to the previously played strings.

The very first tone produced by a full length string has a name — “the fundamental” or “first partial”. The second tone produced by a string half in length is called the “second partial” or “first overtone”. The third tone is called the “third partial” or “second overtone”. Relative to the second partial (or first overtone), it is up a 5th interval. The fourth partial produces a tone that is up a perfect 4th from the previous partial (the second overtone).

playing The overtone Series on the guitar

Here is how you play the first 3 overtones of the Harmonic Series.

  1. The open E string is the fundamental (or 1st partial).
  2. The harmonic at the 12th fret is the 1st overtone (or 2nd partial). It is up an octave (an 8th) interval and therefore it is the note E.
  3. The harmonic at the 7th fret is the 2nd overtone (or 3rd partial). It is up a fifth interval relative to the previous note and therefore it is the note B.
  4. And the harmonic at the 5th fret is the 3rd overtone (or 4th partial). It is up a fourth interval relative to the previous note and therefore it is the note E.

To play natural harmonics, lightly touch the low E string without fretting the string and pluck relatively forcefully for a full sound. You’ll play harmonics on the 12th, 7th, and 5th frets.

You may also have inadvertently discovered the overtone series when tuning your guitar. Other strings will sympathetically vibrate when you pluck the low E string. Sometimes your tuner will jump around and display notes other than “E”. These other mysterious notes are simply the tones from the harmonic series (overtone series). You can help your tuner out by muting the other strings with your free hand.

Music Theory: The Perfect Intervals

13

Each interval has 2 attributes:

  1. Quantity. This is the number of note names the interval spans. This is what we covered in detail in Chapter 11, “Intro To Intervals.”
  2. Quality. Quality describes the number of half steps traversed from the lower note to the higher note in the interval. The term that represents this number will come before the interval name. For example, “Perfect 4th”. “Perfect” represents the quality of the 4th interval. There are five different interval qualities—Major, Minor, Augmented, Diminished, and Perfect. In this book, we are only focusing on the perfect interval quality because it will help us understand Power Chords (chapter 15).

 

There are 4 intervals in the “Perfect” family: Unisons, 4ths, 5ths, and 8ths (also called the octave).

The Perfect Unison Interval

In theory, the Unison interval might be a little more complicated than it is in the real world. This interval is the same note. For example, when you and a friend both play the E string, you are playing “in unison”. The distance from E to E is 0 (no half steps traversed) and only 1 note name is counted (just E). Therefore the interval is a called a Perfect Unison. The shorthand for this interval is P1.

The Perfect 4th Interval

A 4th interval is perfect if you traverse 5 half steps. For example, the interval from G to C is a 4th because it spans 4 note names. G (1), A (2), B (3), and C (4). Its quality is Perfect because you traverse 5 half steps. Therefore G–C is a Perfect 4th. The shorthand for this interval is P4.

The Perfect 5th Interval

A 5th interval is perfect if you traverse 7 half steps. For example, C to G is a Perfect 5th. The shorthand for this interval is P5.

The Perfect 8th Interval

And finally an octave interval (an 8th) is perfect if you traverse 12 half steps. For example, from the low C to the high C is an octave (or Perfect 8th). The shorthand for this interval is P8. As you know, this interval is also called the octave.

Music Theory: Why B–F is not a Perfect 5th

17

The interval B–F spans 5 note names. B (1), C (2), D (3), E (4), and F (5). Therefore, it is indeed a 5th interval.

But it’s a “Diminished 5th” not a “Perfect 5th” because you only need to traverse 6 half-steps from B to F. Take the distance you need to go up the fretboard from E to B (7 frets) and compare it to the distance you need to go from B to F (just 6 frets). (As discussed in previous chapters, E–B is a Perfect 5th interval.)

You can also use a single line of notes to count the half steps from B to F (which is 6) and from E to B (7). Remember to start counting from zero. B (0), C (1), unnamed black note (2), D (3), unnamed black note (4), E (5), and finally F (6).

In summary, in order for a 5th to be Perfect, you must traverse 7 half-steps. Therefore B–F is called a “Diminished 5th”.

Music Theory: Inverting the Perfect Intervals

21

The Perfect Interval Family is:

  1. The Perfect Unison. It spans 1 note name and you traverse 0 half steps from the root tone to the next note in the interval..
  2. The Perfect 4th. It spans 4 note names and you traverse 5 half steps from the root tone to the next note in the interval..
  3. The Perfect 5th. It spans 5 note names and you traverse 7 half steps from the root tone to the next note in the interval..
  4. The Perfect 8th (or Octave). It spans 8 note names and you traverse 12 half steps from the root tone to the next note in the interval.

In this chapter, we’ll discuss what it means to “invert” an interval. There are only two notes in an interval—the lower note and the higher 2nd note. To invert an interval, simply change the position of the notes so the lower note now becomes the higher note.

G5 is G–D. The lower note is G. The higher note is D. To invert it, take the G and make it the high note. So now it is D–G. The low note is D. The high note is G.

Take a moment and analyze the interval D–G. How many note names does it span?

It spans 4 notes. Notice that I’ve edited the 12 notes in such a way so that resembles the piano. It puts the notes of the scale next to each other suggesting that when you count notes spanned in an interval, you don’t have to worry about counting half steps. Just count the letter names spanned.

 

How many half steps must you traverse to get from D to G? When counting half steps, you must count every note—including the out-of-key black notes.

So what kind of interval is it? It is a Perfect 4th!

  1. When you invert a Perfect 5th, it becomes a Perfect 4th interval.
  2. When you invert a Perfect 4th, it then become a Perfect 5th interval.

 

Inverting a Diminished 5th (like B–F) creates the interval F–B. The half-steps traversed from F to B is 6 but a Perfect 4th is only 5 half-steps. Therefore the 4th, F–B, is a little larger and therefore we call it an “Augmented 4th”.

Some Important Terms

29

To get started with learning how to read standard notation, there are a few terms we need to define.

Beat = the pulse of music

The beat of music is like your heart’s pulse. It is steady and consistent.  If you listen to any song, you should be able to tap your foot to the beat of the song.

 

Quarter note = 1 beat of music

Written notes in sheet music not only tell you the pitch (ie. which fret of the guitar) but also how long you should play that note. The time it takes from one beat of music to occur up until the very next beat is exactly how long a quarter note should be played. The easiest quarter note to play is the one that is synced with the beat of the music.

 

Music Staff

The music staff has 5 lines and 4 spaces. Higher pitches are at the top of the staff. Lower pitches are at the bottom of the staff.

 

Bar Lines (or measure lines)

Bar lines (the vertical lines seen below) divide the music staff into measures. When music is divided into measures, it makes it easy to communicate with your bandmates where you want to start or end because they are often numbered.

 

Measures

Each measure contains a certain amount of beats. A common amount is simply 4 beats in a single measure. However, we will also learn to read music that has 2 beats in a measure and 3 beats in a measure. The quarter note gets its name because it’s very common for music to have 4 beats in a measure and therefore each beat is a quarter of the entire measure 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 + 1/4 = 1 (or the whole measure). So in this case, if a note = beat, then it is a quarter note.

 

Time Signature

There are two aspects to a time signature. First, the “time signature” tells us how many beats are in each measure. Again, it’s common to have just 4 but it’s possible to have 2, 3, 5, 6, or 7 beats in a measure too.

Second, the time signature tells us which type of note gets the pulse of the music. Again, the most common is the quarter note = 1 beat of music.

The top number = number of beats per measure (4). Bottom number = the type of note that gets the beat (1/4 note).

 

Double Bar

A double bar indicates the end of a piece of music or a transition within the music. The double bar is on the far right.

 

Counting

Here is a piece of music that lacks pitch and only indicates rhythm. With this type of sheet music, you can play a single note, a single drum, or other percussion instruments. Its time signature is “4/4” which means there are 4 beats per measure and that the quarter note gets the beat. Above each note, I’ve indicated how you should “count” the music. Notice that you start over with each measure. Feel free to clap this rhythm and count as you clap. Are you able to do so with a steady and consistent pulse?

Now, try playing the above with the just open E string. Tap your foot and play the open E string at the same time. Are you able to play with a steady pulse? Are you able to play the E string on the guitar and tap your foot at the same time? These are skills that I learned to do in elementary school on my first instrument (the saxophone). And so I believe that all guitarists should learn to tap their foot while the play. It will help you develop an internal sense of rhythm.

 

The Treble Clef

There are a variety of “clefs” and their job is to tell us which notes the spaces and lines represent. The Treble Clef symbol (at the very far left) tells us that the lines of the music staff represent the notes (from bottom to top): E, G, B, D, and F. It also tells us that the spaces of the music staff represent the notes (from bottom to top): F, A, C, and E.

I would bet that you are familiar with the song “Do-Re-Mi” from the musical “The Sound of Music”. Even if you haven’t watched the movie, chances are you know the catchy tune: “Do, a deer, a female deer / Re, a drop of golden sun…”

While the lyrics may seem lighthearted and reminiscent of a nursery rhyme, they actually serve a significant purpose in teaching the fundamental musical skill known as solfège.

“Solfège” is heavily used in choir classes and private singing lessons. It is the tool that music teachers use to help the student develop their “musical ear”. However, many music students who go through a typical American education never learn how to develop their musical ear. Instead, their teachers require them to be very good note readers and performers. Of course there’s nothing wrong with performing written music from a page. But the most important aspect of becoming a musician is learning how to audiate. Audiation is the ability to produce the music on your instrument that you can imagine or “hear” in your head.

Solfège is one of the primary tools that you will use to learn how to audiate. I will provide you with some practical exercises that you can start incorporating into your daily routine.

Solfège is a naming system that assigns a unique name to each note in a scale. It utilizes a method called solmization, which assigns a single syllable (such as Do or Re) to each scale degree. These syllables differ from the actual note names (such as C or D).

The most commonly used solfège scale in music education is the major scale, which consists of the syllables Do, Re, Mi, Fa, Sol, La, Ti, and Do again. Because these syllables match the scale degree, they can be applied to any major scale, regardless of the key. For example, both the C major scale and the E major scale would use the same solfège syllables in the exact same order. In the key of C major, Do represents the note C, whereas in the key of E major, Do represents the note E. In C major, Re represents the note D. In E major it represents the note F♯ (“F-sharp”).

“Do” changes depending on the key you are in, hence the term “movable Do”. This concept is beneficial as it allows you to internalize the relative nature of every scale without having to sing every scale.

How To Do The Exercises

Each exercise has a high part and a low part. The high part is for people who are sopranos or altos. The low part is for people who are tenors, baritones, or basses.

 

  1. Play the guitar part that is appropriate for your voice—either the top line or the bottom line. Play those few notes and memorize the melody.
  2. Then do your best to sing the notes without playing at all. At first, you will be off. That is okay. You must learn to sing without using a crutch. The more you exercise this “audiation” muscle, the better you will get at it.
  3. However, for the “Do – Mi – Sol” exercises, I included the C Major chord for you to play to accompany yourself because you will be singing the chord tones of that chord. It’s important to learn how to sing one of the notes in a chord and get a sense of how that feels.
  4. If you absolutely must, play the guitar and sing the notes at the same time (in unison). Try to avoid doing that but it’s understandable if you need to do that at first.
  5. If you cannot match the pitch on the guitar, see if you can find a friend to sing the note. Then try to match their voice. Sometimes it can be easier to match the pitch from someone else’s voice than from an instrument.

Going back to the Harmonic (Overtone) Series, the next two intervals that occur are the Major 3rd and Minor 3rd.

You can hear the interval jump by playing higher up the harmonic series on the low E string of the guitar. Fret “3.2” is just beyond the 3rd fret.

The 3rd Interval is part of the “Imperfect Family” of intervals. 2nds, 3rds, 6ths, and 7ths are all imperfect intervals. Whereas Unisons, 4ths, 5ths, and 8ths are part of the “Perfect Interval Family”. We’ll start with discussing the 3rd interval because the G string and the B string are a 3rd interval apart. Therefore it’s important we explore some of the interval and chord shapes that happen on the treble strings (string set 3-2-1).

The imperfect intervals are typically thought of as either “Major” or “Minor”. So what is the difference between the “Major 3rd” and the “Minor 3rd”? As we know from Level 1, the number of letter names spanned in an interval is its quantity (the general classification). Therefore both the Major and Minor 3rd intervals must span 3 note names.

However the interval quality is not the same between the two. The interval quality of the Major 3rd, being the number of half steps traversed, is bigger than the Minor 3rd. You must traverse 4 half steps in a Major 3rd but only 3 half steps in a Minor 3rd.

The first Major 3rd interval we’ll look at is G–B. G–B spans 3 note names— G (1), A (2), and B (3). And you must traverse 4 half steps from G to B— G, G♯(1), A (2), A♯(3), B (4). You can verify this on the G string (3rd string) on the guitar. The 4th fret of the G string is the note B.

Typically, the 3rd fret of any guitar will have a dot inlay fret marker. This particular fret marker indicates the Minor 3rd interval relative to each open string. On the E strings, the 3rd fret is the note G. E–G is a Minor 3rd interval because it spans 3 note names and you only need to traverse 3 half steps—E, F (1), F♯(2), G (3).

Major is bigger than Minor. Or Major = Big and Minor = Little.

Think of the Big Dipper and Little Dipper asterisms (which are recognizable patterns within a constellation). The Big Dipper is part of Ursa Major constellation and the Little Dipper is part of Ursa Minor constellation.

Tonic vs. Root. Why do I use the term "Tonic"?

2

In school, I’ve been taught that Tonic refers to the first note of the scale. And “root” refers to the foundational note of a chord.

But many people use “root” to refer to both (first note of a scale and the foundational note of a chord). I think this confuses beginners. I recommend that teachers use the correct terminology and stop using root to refer to the first note of a scale. Here are 6 reasons why.

 

  1. Musicians have used tonic to uniquely identify the first note of a scale for hundreds of years. Certainly, beginners have had no issues with that term prior to the invention of the internet. The book Elson’s Pocket Music Dictionary was published in 1909 and correctly defines each term.
    • “Tonic. The key-note of any scale; the chief, fundamental ground-tone, or first note, of the scale.” (p. 149)
    • “Root. The fundamental note of any chord.” (p. 126)
  2. Beginners actually get confused when you use root to both uniquely identify the first note of the scale and as the fundamental note of a chord. When beginners see a chord chart with a simple progression like 1-4-5 in the key of C, they will sometimes ask “For the C Major chord, do I play the C Major scale? And then for the F Major chord, do I play the F Major scale? And then for the G Major chord, do I play the G Major scale?”
    • This is obviously wrong. But how can you blame them? They’re only being logical, in my opinion.
  3. There are lots of terms in music that are much more confusing. There’s no need to try to change just 1 word when we use thousands of other complex jargon-y terms. It’s a better strategy to find a way to teach beginners so that they understand music theory with the correct terminology.
  4. Not one person who consistently uses the word “root” has ever told me that I should stop using the word tonic. Because they know that I’m not wrong. I’m using the correct terminology that is used in schools, on the AP Music Theory test, and at colleges and universities.
  5. Every note is a root note! When you understand that a root note is the foundational or fundamental note for a chord, then it doesn’t make sense to uniquely identify the 1st note of a scale as “the root”.
  6. Every scale degree has a name! Tonic, Supertonic, Mediant, Subdominant, Dominant, Submediant, and Leading Tone. Nobody has ever suggested we stop using the other 6 names. Why are people misusing just one of them—the tonic—and not the rest?

I think people use root for both because they say that chords are built from scales. This isn’t entirely correct. Yes, you can use a scale to quickly identify chord tones. But the truth is that both chords and scales are built from intervals. A Major Triad is found in a variety of keys and modes (Major, Minor, etc) and is built with the formula: a Major 3rd + a Minor 3rd. In a Major key, you’ll find the Major Triad built from the 1st, 4th, and 5th scale degrees. A Major key is Major because the tonic chord is Major.

Maybe everyone should start using the term key-note instead? I think it’s great! It tells you that the key is named after this note. And everyone knows that the key-note speaker or presentation is the first one to happen! Or just use Tonic. Either way, I think using “root” to refer to both concepts hinders students’ understanding of music because eventually they will learn about “tonic” and will wonder why you never used this term.
